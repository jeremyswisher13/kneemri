## Course Overview

**Description:** A primary care sports medicine elbow MRI course focused on management-changing interpretation: the thrower's elbow (UCL valgus overload, capitellar OCD, ulnar neuritis, olecranon stress), the LCL complex and posterolateral rotatory instability, the coronoid/anteromedial-facet axis, the distal biceps and triceps, lateral and medial epicondylitis, the three nerves, and don't-miss findings.

### Search Pattern

#### 1. Verify Protocol & Clinical Question

Confirm side, protocol, and the planes/sequences you actually have, then let the symptom location steer the read before you narrow your attention.

Checklist:
- Confirm correct patient, side, and any comparison / contralateral imaging
- Identify protocol: non-contrast vs MR arthrogram; is a FABS view present for the distal biceps?
- Inventory planes and sequences (axial, coronal, sagittal, FABS; T1, PD/T2 FS, STIR) and note artifact
- State the exact question: UCL vs medial epicondylitis vs ulnar nerve vs OCD vs distal biceps vs post-trauma instability
- Map the symptom: medial (valgus triad), lateral (epicondylitis / LCL / PLRI), anterior (biceps), posterior (triceps / olecranon / valgus-extension overload), or mechanical (loose body / plica)
Pearls:
- Coronal answers the UCL, the epicondyle origins, and the radiocapitellar (OCD) face; axial answers the nerves; sagittal answers loose bodies and the anterior-to-posterior extent of an OCD. Confirm a finding in ≥2 planes.
- MR arthrography earns its place only when it will change the decision — confirming a partial undersurface UCL tear (T-sign), judging OCD stability, or counting loose bodies. Non-contrast 3T answers most cases.

#### 2. Bones, Marrow & Osteochondral

Sweep the radial head, capitellum, trochlea, olecranon, and coronoid first — T1 for marrow replacement, fluid-sensitive for edema and fracture lines. Tie every edema signal to a line, physis, cyst, or fragment.

Checklist:
- Radial head, capitellum, trochlea, olecranon, coronoid: marrow edema tied to a fracture line, physis, cyst, or fragment
- Capitellar OCD vs Panner; judge OCD stability (fluid undercutting the fragment, sizeable or multiple subchondral cysts, cartilage breach, displaced fragment) — read on CORONAL + SAGITTAL together
- Thrower stress: medial epicondyle apophysis (Little League elbow), olecranon stress reaction vs fracture / persistent physis, posteromedial olecranon (valgus-extension-overload) osteophyte
- Occult fracture (posterior fat-pad sign; lateral condyle in children); use CRITOE and the radiocapitellar line
- Sweep the coronoid + olecranon fossae and the radiocapitellar recess for loose bodies; in a non-thrower, consider primary OA as the source
Pearls:
- Stability changes management in capitellar OCD: a T2-bright line undercutting the fragment, a sizeable/multiple subchondral cysts, a breached cartilage surface, or a displaced fragment/loose body all argue unstable → surgical referral. (The 5 mm cyst figure is a knee-derived criterion, not validated for the capitellum — teach 'sizeable or multiple.')
- Panner disease is the younger child (≈5–10 yr), is reversible, and spares the surface; OCD is the older adolescent thrower/gymnast with a discrete fragment that can become unstable.

#### 3. Medial Ligament — UCL & Valgus Overload

Trace the anterior bundle of the UCL from the medial epicondyle to the sublime tubercle, then read it together with the flexor-pronator origin and the ulnar nerve — the medial 'valgus triad.'

Checklist:
- Anterior bundle from the medial epicondyle to the sublime tubercle on coronal
- Partial undersurface tear (T-sign: fluid tracking beyond the articular-cartilage edge at the distal attachment) vs full-thickness; record location proximal / mid / distal
- Chronic attenuation, thickening, or ossification; sublime-tubercle marrow edema or a traction spur
- Link to the flexor-pronator origin and the ulnar nerve — valgus overload injures all three together
Pearls:
- The T-sign is the partial undersurface tear — fluid/contrast undercuts the most distal fibers at the sublime tubercle. A couple of millimetres of undercutting can be a normal recess; MR arthrography is more sensitive than non-contrast for this.
- In a thrower, every medial-pain read is UCL + flexor-pronator origin + ulnar nerve. A concomitant UCL tear shifts a 'medial epicondylitis' diagnosis toward UCL reconstruction.

#### 4. Lateral Ligament — LCL Complex, PLRI & Coronoid

Trace the lateral ulnar collateral ligament and the radial collateral / annular ligaments, then read the coronoid and radial head as a post-traumatic circle. Comment on LUCL integrity in EVERY post-dislocation or post-lateral-surgery elbow.

Checklist:
- LUCL (epicondyle → posterior to the radial head → supinator crest), RCL, and annular ligament; trace on coronal + axial
- Humeral-origin avulsion and the undersurface tear deep to the common extensor; secondary PLRI signs (posterolateral radial subluxation, radiocapitellar incongruity)
- Coronoid: the anteromedial-facet fracture (small but unstable → varus posteromedial rotatory instability) and Regan–Morrey height; the terrible-triad pattern
- Post-dislocation Osborne–Cotterill posterolateral capitellar impaction and heterotopic ossification
Pearls:
- A deep/full-thickness common-extensor tear that undercuts toward the radiocapitellar joint and involves the LUCL implies posterolateral rotatory instability → this is a ligament repair/reconstruction problem, not just tendinosis (and a known complication of prior lateral steroid injection or release).
- Small does not mean stable at the coronoid: the anteromedial-facet fracture implies varus posteromedial rotatory instability and is surgical; the terrible triad (dislocation + radial head + coronoid) hinges on the coronoid and the LCL.

#### 5. Tendons — Biceps, Triceps & Epicondyle Origins

Grade the distal biceps and triceps for partial vs complete, and the common flexor and extensor origins for tendinosis vs tear — always tracing the adjacent ligament (UCL with medial, LUCL with lateral).

Checklist:
- Distal biceps (best on FABS): partial vs complete, retraction in cm, lacertus fibrosus intact vs torn; bicipitoradial bursitis as a clue
- Triceps: partial (deep-fiber, report % width) vs complete avulsion ± an olecranon flake fragment
- Lateral (ECRB) epicondylitis grade — trace the LUCL to the supinator crest; medial (flexor-pronator) epicondylitis grade — trace the UCL
- Apply a grade 1 / 2 / 3 muscle-strain framework to flexor-pronator and extensor strains
Pearls:
- An empty radial tuberosity with retraction is a complete distal biceps tear — but a complete tear with an INTACT lacertus fibrosus may NOT retract and is the classic mimic; comment on the lacertus, because it informs the surgical approach. Repair timing is a guideline (earlier is technically easier), not a hard deadline.
- Don't undercall the triceps: a complete rupture is surgical, and a high-grade partial (commonly >50% width, or any tear with extension weakness against resistance) is repaired.

#### 6. Nerves — Ulnar, Median / AIN, Radial / PIN

Read the three nerves by caliber + signal against an internal control, and let muscle denervation be the localizing secondary sign. Anchor the diagnosis to a structural change, not signal alone.

Checklist:
- Ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel: caliber/signal vs the contralateral side and the ipsilateral median nerve; flexion subluxation; anconeus epitrochlearis; snapping triceps
- Median / AIN: isolated pronator-quadratus / FPL / FDP (index–middle) denervation — consider Parsonage–Turner before calling mechanical compression
- Radial / PIN at the arcade of Frohse: extensor-compartment denervation (supinator variably spared); radial tunnel = pain with a normal MRI
- Use muscle denervation edema (subacute) and fatty atrophy (chronic) as the localizing sign; hunt a mass/ganglion as the cause
Pearls:
- A structural cause changes the operation: dynamic ulnar-nerve subluxation, an anconeus epitrochlearis, or snapping triceps favor transposition (and the triceps must be addressed) over in-situ decompression.
- An AIN palsy is frequently neuralgic amyotrophy (Parsonage–Turner), not mechanical compression → usually no surgery; pivot to EMG/observation. Denervation shows no MRI change for the first ~2–4 weeks, then T2/STIR edema, then chronic T1 fatty atrophy.

#### 7. Cartilage, Joint, Capsule, Bursa & Synovium

Trace the radiocapitellar and ulnotrochlear cartilage, characterize effusion/synovitis, and clear the dependent recesses for loose bodies — watching for the plica that mimics them.

Checklist:
- Radiocapitellar and ulnotrochlear cartilage; effusion and synovitis
- Radiocapitellar plica / posterolateral synovial fold (pathologic when thickened beyond ~3 mm with mechanical symptoms) as a mimic of a loose body or OCD
- Olecranon bursitis — aseptic vs septic (aspirate the warm bursa; MRI cannot exclude infection)
- Loose bodies in the dependent recesses; primary OA osteophytes at the coronoid and olecranon tips
Pearls:
- A red/warm/fluctuant olecranon bursa needs aspiration, not just imaging — MRI cannot reliably exclude a septic bursa.
- The radiocapitellar plica is a real mechanical cause of lateral catching, but it is also a loose-body/OCD mimic — call it pathologic only when it's thickened (>~3 mm) with concordant mechanical symptoms.

#### 8. Masses, Management Review & Red Flags

Before you sign off, hunt a mass as the hidden cause, exclude the infection/tumor red flags, and name the single management-changing finding.

Checklist:
- Masses / ganglia (often the real entrapment cause); a distended bicipitoradial bursa compressing the PIN
- Confluent low-T1 marrow replacement, cortical destruction, or a soft-tissue mass: infection / tumor red flags
- Confirm any surgically urgent injury (complete biceps / triceps / UCL / LUCL avulsion, anteromedial coronoid facet, an instability pattern)
- Summarize the single management-changing finding: rehab/injection, surgical referral, or urgent escalation
Pearls:
- When a nerve looks denervated without an obvious tunnel cause, look one more time for a ganglion or mass — it is frequently the surgically treatable cause.
- End every read with the disposition, not just the finding: what does this change — continue rehab, inject, refer, or escalate urgently?

#### Topic 1: Plane-to-structure map: axial = nerves/origins/biceps footprint, coronal = UCL/LCL/epicondyles/radiocapitellar, sagittal = fossae/triceps

### Plane-to-Structure Map: Axial, Coronal, Sagittal

The first orientation skill is knowing **which plane answers which question** — because most elbow structures have one plane that displays them in profile and others that average through them.

| Plane | Best for | The clinical question it answers |
|---|---|---|
| **Axial** | Nerves (ulnar in the cubital tunnel, radial/PIN at the radiocapitellar level), common flexor/extensor origins in cross-section, the **annular ligament** around the radial neck, the distal biceps footprint | "Is there ulnar neuritis or flexion subluxation?" "Where is the entrapment?" |
| **Coronal** | The **UCL anterior bundle** to the sublime tubercle, the RCL/LUCL complex, the common flexor and extensor origins, and the **radiocapitellar contact zone** | "Is there a UCL tear (the T-sign), epicondylitis, or an LCL-origin tear?" |
| **Sagittal** | Olecranon/coronoid **fossae and the radiocapitellar recess** for loose bodies, the olecranon/triceps insertion, the anterior/posterior fat pads | "Is there a loose body or a triceps tear?" |

**Capitellar OCD is the key cross-plane rule:** read it on **coronal + sagittal together**, not sagittal alone. The coronal shows the lesion at the radiocapitellar surface where it lives; the sagittal defines its anterior-to-posterior extent and contour. A lesion convincing on one plane but absent on the orthogonal plane is more likely a partial-volume artifact than a real defect.

**Course-image note:** the normal elbow workstation now includes coronal, axial, and sagittal stacks, so the course can teach the plane map visually. Dedicated **FABS** views for distal-biceps footprint grading and **MR arthrography** for subtle UCL undersurface tears or equivocal OCD stability remain protocol choices that are not represented by the normal stacks.

**Pearl:** Each elbow plane owns a question: axial for nerves and the biceps footprint, coronal for the UCL and the radiocapitellar OCD face, sagittal for loose bodies and the triceps — and capitellar OCD must be read on coronal AND sagittal together, never sagittal alone.

#### Topic 2: FABS positioning: laying the distal biceps footprint flat for partial-vs-complete grading and retraction

### FABS Positioning: Profiling the Distal Biceps Footprint

The distal biceps tendon runs **obliquely** to its radial-tuberosity footprint, so on standard planes it is foreshortened and partial-volumed — you cannot reliably grade a partial tear or measure retraction. **FABS** solves this.

**FABS = Flexed elbow, ABducted shoulder, forearm Supinated**, with the patient **prone** and the arm overhead. This position lays the distal biceps tendon **flat and in-plane all the way to its radial-tuberosity footprint**, so you can:
- Distinguish **partial** (some fibers still reaching the tuberosity) from **complete** (full discontinuity) tears
- **Measure retraction** — where the stump sits, in cm
- Assess the **bifurcated footprint** (short head distal/anterior, long head proximal/posterior)

**Order FABS when the question is the distal biceps** — a middle-aged patient with a flexion-loading injury, antecubital ecchymosis, weak supination, or a positive hook test. The two management-changing variables FABS lets you report are **partial-vs-complete** and **retraction**, the features that drive repair urgency. (The lacertus-fibrosus status, the other key variable, is read on axial.)

**Pitfall to avoid:** FABS is for the **distal biceps**, not the UCL — don't confuse the two when selecting positioning.

**Pearl:** When the distal biceps is the question, add the FABS view (Flexed elbow, ABducted shoulder, Supinated forearm, prone) — it lays the tendon flat to its radial-tuberosity footprint so you can grade partial-vs-complete and measure retraction, which standard planes foreshorten.

#### Topic 3: MR arthrography vs non-contrast: the T-sign, loose bodies, and when contrast changes the operation

### MR Arthrography vs Non-Contrast: When Contrast Changes the Operation

**Non-contrast 3T MRI is the default** and answers most elbow questions — tendons, OCD, nerves, occult fracture, loose bodies. **Direct MR arthrography** (intra-articular dilute gadolinium) earns its needle only when the added detail will change a decision.

**MR arthrography is the most sensitive study for:**
- The **partial undersurface UCL tear — the T-sign**: intra-articular contrast undercuts the deep fibers at the sublime tubercle, making an articular-sided partial tear conspicuous that non-contrast MRI undercalls. This is the classic throwing injury.
- **Intra-articular loose bodies and chondral delamination/flaps**: contrast outlines bodies and tracks under a delaminated flap.

**Add it when:**
- A thrower's exam says UCL but **non-contrast MRI is equivocal**
- **OCD stability** will change the surgical decision
- You need to **count loose bodies** pre-op (CT/CT arthrography is also strong for ossified bodies)

**The shoulder-ABER caveat (don't over-promise):** an abduction/valgus-applied or flexed positioning adjunct *can* open the medial joint to improve undersurface-tear conspicuity, but the **elbow evidence base is thin** compared with the shoulder ABER. Present it as an **adjunct, not a validated routine sequence**.

**Course-image caveat:** the local normal stacks are non-arthrographic, so the T-sign is taught as a concept with the explicit reminder that **MR arthrography is more sensitive** when the clinical question is a subtle undersurface UCL tear.

**Pearl:** Reach for MR arthrography only when it changes the operation — confirming the partial undersurface UCL T-sign, detecting/counting loose bodies, or settling OCD stability when non-contrast is equivocal; non-contrast 3T answers most elbow cases, and an elbow valgus/ABER adjunct is not a validated routine.

#### Topic 4: Sequences: fluid-sensitive fat-sat for edema/tears vs T1 for marrow/anatomy; confirm in ≥2 planes

### Sequences: Fluid-Sensitive Fat-Sat vs T1 — and Confirming in ≥2 Planes

Match the **sequence to the tissue question**, exactly as in the other joints.

| Sequence | Reads | Use it for |
|---|---|---|
| **Fluid-sensitive fat-sat** (PD/T2 FS or **STIR**) | **Fluid + edema** | Tendon/ligament tears, the **OCD fluid rim**, marrow injury/stress, effusions, and **subacute muscle denervation edema** |
| **T1** | **Marrow, fat, anatomy** | Marrow **replacement** (confluent low-T1 below muscle = red flag), arthrogram contrast, occult fracture lines, chronic **fatty muscle atrophy** |

Think **T1 = anatomy/marrow, fluid-sensitive = pathology/edema.** A fracture line is a low-signal line on T1 with **fat-suppressed-bright** marrow edema around it; a bone *bruise* shows edema **without** a line.

**The orientation discipline: confirm every finding in at least two planes.** A signal abnormality on one plane that disappears on the orthogonal plane is usually **partial-volume averaging or a normal contour**, not pathology. This is why the OCD rule (coronal + sagittal) and the LUCL rule (coronal + axial) exist — partial-volume averaging is the single most common reason a real lesion is missed *or* a normal variant is over-called.

**Available in the normal workstation:** **coronal T2-FS**, **axial T2-FS**, and **sagittal IR/STIR** stacks. They are enough to teach orientation and two-plane confirmation, while T1, MR arthrography, and FABS remain protocol-specific additions when the clinical question requires them.

**Pearl:** Fluid-sensitive fat-sat shows the pathology (tears, edema, the OCD rim) and T1 shows the anatomy and the marrow red flag — and a finding seen on only one plane is partial-volume artifact until you confirm it on a second.

#### Topic 5: Normal-variant pitfalls: the capitellar pseudodefect, transverse trochlear ridge, and distal-UCL synovial recess

### Normal-Variant Pitfalls — Don't Call Them Lesions

Three normal findings are routinely over-called at the elbow. Recognizing them is part of orientation, because each lives exactly where a real lesion would and each sends a normal elbow toward unnecessary imaging or referral.

| Variant | Location | How to call it normal |
|---|---|---|
| **Pseudodefect of the capitellum** | **Posterolateral / postero-inferior** bare-area groove at the capitellum–lateral-epicondyle junction | A normal contour notch where the articular cartilage ends posteriorly — **not** an osteochondral lesion. True capitellar OCD is **anterolateral/central** (the radiocapitellar contact zone), with a fluid rim, cyst, or fragment |
| **Transverse trochlear ridge / trochlear groove** | Trochlea | A normal bony ridge/groove in the trochlear contour — not an osteochondral defect |
| **Normal distal-UCL synovial recess** | Just distal to the UCL attachment at the sublime tubercle | A small synovial recess can normally exist; the abnormal **T-sign** threshold is fluid/contrast tracking **beyond the articular-cartilage edge**, not a couple of millimeters undercutting the most distal fibers |

**The location lesson:** for capitellar OCD, the real lesion is **anterolateral/central** and the mimic (pseudodefect) is **posterolateral** — location separates variant from pathology. For the UCL, a normal recess sits at the attachment but a true partial tear has contrast extending **past the cartilage margin**. Anchor each call to **location + a corroborating sign** (fluid rim, cyst, fragment, contrast beyond the cartilage edge), not to signal alone.

**Pearl:** The capitellar pseudodefect is a posterolateral bare-area groove — true OCD is anterolateral/central — and a small distal-UCL synovial recess is normal unless fluid tracks beyond the articular-cartilage edge; location plus a corroborating sign, not signal alone, separates variant from lesion.

#### Topic 1: Why a fixed, bones-first order beats lesion-spotting

### Why a Fixed, Bones-First Order Beats Lesion-Spotting

The natural instinct is to **chase the obvious finding** — the thickened, bright common-extensor origin in a tennis player, the fluid-filled olecranon bursa — and then quit. That is **satisfaction of search**, and at the elbow it is precisely how the management-changing lesion gets missed: the eye that locks onto lateral epicondylitis glides right past the **deep common-extensor/LUCL tear** sitting underneath it, and the elbow that should have gone to a ligament surgeon goes to rehab instead.

A reproducible order forces you to **visit every compartment regardless of where your attention is pulled**, so the second — often more dangerous — finding still gets seen.

**Why it works:**
- **Coverage, not recall** — you check a structure because it is next on the list, not because you happened to remember it.
- **Calibrates attention** — the high-consequence compartment (**bones/marrow**) is read *early*, while attention is freshest, not last when fatigue sets in.
- **Auditable dictation** — the report writes itself in a fixed sequence reviewers can follow.

**The elbow caveat on planes:** the normal workstation includes coronal, axial, and sagittal stacks, which is enough to teach the compartment map and the discipline of naming what each plane can and cannot answer. A complete pathology read still flags when a dedicated **FABS** view (distal biceps) or **MR arthrogram** (subtle UCL undersurface tear, equivocal OCD stability, or loose-body/chondral mapping) would change the decision.

**Management impact:** lesion-spotting over-calls the symptomatic structure and under-calls the silent surgical one. A fixed order keeps you from discharging an elbow to a counterforce brace when the marrow held an unstable OCD or the LUCL was torn.

**Pearl:** The first finding you see is rarely the one that changes disposition — a fixed, bones-first order exists precisely to make you keep looking after you have already found something.

#### Topic 2: The eight-step elbow search pattern

### The Eight-Step Elbow Search Pattern

Drive every study through the same sequence. **Context and bones/marrow go first** (steps 1-2), while attention is freshest, because that is where the disposition-changing lesions hide. **Confirm every finding in ≥2 planes** and pair **T1** (marrow, fat, occult fracture lines, arthrogram contrast) with **fluid-sensitive fat-sat** (PD/T2 FS or STIR for edema, fluid, tears, denervation).

| Step | Region | High-yield target |
|---|---|---|
| **1 Verify** | Protocol & question | Side, non-contrast vs arthrogram, FABS present?; planes/sequences on hand; map the symptom (medial/lateral/anterior/posterior/mechanical) |
| **2 Bones/Marrow** | Capitellum, radial head, trochlea, olecranon, coronoid | **Unstable OCD vs Panner**, olecranon stress line, occult radial-head fracture, CRITOE, radiocapitellar line, marrow replacement |
| **3 Medial ligament** | UCL anterior bundle | Sublime-tubercle attachment, **T-sign** (fluid beyond the cartilage edge), proximal/mid/distal, chronic spur |
| **4 Lateral ligament** | LCL/LUCL, RCL, annular | **LUCL to the supinator crest**, undersurface tear deep to common extensor, **anteromedial coronoid facet**, PLRI signs |
| **5 Tendons** | Biceps, triceps, flexor/extensor origins | Biceps partial-vs-complete + lacertus, triceps % width, epicondylitis grade |
| **6 Nerves** | Ulnar, median/AIN, radial/PIN | Caliber/signal vs contralateral & median internal control, flexion subluxation, denervation pattern |
| **7 Joint/Synovium** | Cartilage, effusion, bursa, recesses | Loose bodies in dependent recesses, radiocapitellar plica, septic vs aseptic bursa |
| **8 Review/Red flags** | Masses + final management | Confluent low-T1 replacement, confirm the urgent injury, state the single disposition |

**Bones at step 2, not last** — the unstable OCD, the posteromedial olecranon stress fracture, and the occult radial-head fracture are the findings that change today's plan.

**Pearl:** Run bones/marrow as step 2, not last: the unstable OCD, the olecranon stress fracture, and the occult radial-head fracture are disposition-changers that get missed to end-of-read fatigue.

#### Topic 3: Satisfaction-of-search traps: the epicondylitis that hides a ligament

### Satisfaction-of-Search Traps: The Epicondylitis That Hides a Ligament

Two over-learned diagnoses — lateral and medial epicondylitis — are the elbow's most reliable satisfaction-of-search bait. Each sits **directly superficial to a ligament whose injury is the surgical problem**, so calling the tendinosis and stopping is how the operative lesion gets buried.

**Lateral pain → don't stop at the common extensor.** The **RCL/LUCL origin lies deep to the common extensor tendon**, so a **deep undersurface tear** there hides beneath what looks like simple "tennis elbow." A deep/full-thickness common-extensor tear with a **fluid-bright gap that undercuts or communicates with the radiocapitellar joint and involves the LUCL implies PLRI** — a surgical problem and a classic complication of prior **lateral steroid injection or release**. Trace the LUCL to the **supinator crest** before signing off any high-grade lateral epicondylitis. Conversely, do **not** over-call reactive lateral-epicondyle marrow edema or a thin RCL signal as an LCL tear.

**Medial pain → don't stop at the common flexor.** The **UCL** runs deep to the flexor-pronator origin, and the **ulnar nerve sits immediately posterior** in the cubital tunnel. Medial epicondylitis and UCL injury frequently coexist, especially in throwers, and a concomitant UCL tear shifts a thrower's conversation toward reconstruction.

| You see | You must also read | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Common-extensor tendinosis | **LUCL to the supinator crest** | Deep undersurface tear → PLRI (surgical) |
| Common-flexor tendinosis | **UCL to the sublime tubercle + ulnar nerve** | Coexistent UCL tear / ulnar neuritis changes the plan |

**Defeat tactic:** make the deep ligament a *required* next step the moment you call an epicondylitis — it is on the list, not a matter of memory.

**Pearl:** Read lateral epicondylitis? Trace the LUCL to the supinator crest. Read medial epicondylitis? Trace the UCL and check the ulnar nerve — the adjacent ligament is where the surgery hides.

#### Topic 4: Linked-structure habits: the medial valgus triad and the post-trauma O'Driscoll circle

### Linked-Structure Habits: The Medial Valgus Triad and the Post-Trauma O'Driscoll Circle

Two elbow situations injure structures **as a group**, so reading them as a linked set — not one at a time — is the habit that prevents the second miss.

**The medial valgus triad (every thrower's medial-pain read).** Valgus overload injures three structures together, so read them together:

| Triad member | What to document |
|---|---|
| **UCL anterior bundle** | Sublime-tubercle attachment, T-sign, chronic traction spur |
| **Flexor-pronator origin** | Tendinosis vs partial/full-thickness tear |
| **Ulnar nerve** | Caliber/signal vs contralateral & median; flexion subluxation |

Miss any one and you under-call the injury; the **sublime-tubercle traction spur** is a common bony clue to chronic valgus overload.

**The post-trauma O'Driscoll circle (every dislocation/terrible-triad elbow).** Soft-tissue disruption proceeds **lateral-to-medial** (**LCL/LUCL first → capsule → finally the MCL**), which is why the **LCL is the primary lesion in most simple dislocations** and valgus stability is often preserved. Close the circle in order:

1. **LCL/LUCL** — the usual primary lesion; comment on LUCL integrity explicitly.
2. **Coronoid** — including the **anteromedial facet**, which looks small on radiographs but implies **varus posteromedial rotatory instability (VPMRI)** and is surgical; **small does not mean stable**. (Regan-Morrey grades coronoid *height*; O'Driscoll classifies the *anteromedial facet*.)
3. **Radial head** — the third leg of the **terrible triad** (dislocation + radial-head fracture + coronoid fracture).
4. **Osborne-Cotterill** posterolateral capitellar impaction + posterolateral capsular avulsion — the lateral analog of a Hill-Sachs that perpetuates recurrent PLRI.
5. **Heterotopic ossification** — a common, function-limiting cause of the stiff post-traumatic elbow.

**Management impact:** the coronoid and the LCL are the reconstructive keystones of the terrible triad, and an unflagged anteromedial-facet fracture is the classic don't-miss that drives early arthrosis.

**Pearl:** Medial pain is a triad (UCL + flexor-pronator + ulnar nerve); the dislocated elbow is a circle (LCL/LUCL → coronoid/anteromedial facet → radial head → Osborne-Cotterill → HO) — read the group, not one structure.

#### Topic 5: Sweeping for loose bodies and the plica mimic, then writing a management-grade impression

### Sweeping for Loose Bodies, the Plica Mimic, and Writing a Management-Grade Impression

**The mechanical-symptom sweep.** Any locking/catching history earns a deliberate pass through the three **dependent recesses** where loose bodies settle — the **coronoid fossa, the olecranon fossa, and the radiocapitellar recess** — best seen against bright joint fluid on sagittal fluid-sensitive images (GRE blooms ossified bodies; CT/CT arthrography is most sensitive for counting small ossified bodies pre-op). Don't ascribe every loose body to a throwing lesion: **primary elbow OA** (coronoid/olecranon-tip and fossa osteophytes, chondral loss) is the most common source in the non-thrower/laborer.

**The plica mimic.** A thickened **radiocapitellar plica / posterolateral synovial fold** (pathologic beyond **~3 mm** with mechanical symptoms) causes lateral catching and mimics a loose body or OCD — check for it before you commit.

**Write a management-grade impression.** The read ends in **structured language** that names the structure, the grade, and the consequence — not an anatomic list:

| Axis | Vocabulary |
|---|---|
| Grade | low-grade vs high-grade; partial undersurface vs full-thickness |
| Acuity | acute vs chronic; attenuated vs avulsed |
| Stability | stable vs instability-pattern (PLRI, VPMRI) |
| Nerve | ulnar subluxation / edema / denervation pattern |
| Fragment | displacement in **mm**; intra-articular vs not |

Then **prioritize the single management-changing finding** — rehab/injection, surgical referral, or urgent escalation — rather than burying it. A confluent **low-T1 marrow-replacing** lesion (signal below muscle/disc), a complete biceps/triceps/UCL/LUCL avulsion, an unstable OCD, or an anteromedial coronoid facet fracture each outranks an incidental degenerative finding and leads the impression.

**Pearl:** End every read with a one-line disposition that names the single management-changing finding in structured terms (grade, acuity, stability, mm) — an anatomic list buries the lesion that actually changes the plan.

#### Topic 1: Capitellar OCD vs Panner disease: age, focality, and reversibility

### Capitellar OCD vs Panner Disease

Both are osteochondral lesions of the **capitellum** in young throwers and gymnasts, and both can present with lateral elbow pain and a flexion contracture — but they are different diseases with opposite trajectories. **Three discriminators settle it: age, focality, and reversibility.**

**Panner disease** — a self-limited **osteochondrosis/AVN of the ENTIRE capitellar ossific nucleus** in a younger child (**~5–10 yr**). The whole nucleus looks fragmented and low-signal, but there is **no discrete fragment, no subchondral cyst, and no loose body**. It is **reversible** and managed conservatively — the nucleus remodels and heals.

**Capitellar OCD** — a **FOCAL anterolateral/central osteochondral lesion** of the **adolescent/teen (~12–17 yr)** thrower or gymnast. Unlike Panner, an OCD lesion **can fragment, delaminate, and shed a loose body**, and it does not reliably heal once unstable.

| Feature | Panner | Capitellar OCD |
|---|---|---|
| Age | ~5–10 yr | ~12–17 yr |
| Extent | **Whole** ossific nucleus | **Focal** anterolateral/central |
| Fragment/cyst/loose body | No | Possible |
| Course | Self-limited, reversible | Can fragment; may need surgery |

**Read the lesion on coronal AND sagittal together** — the coronal shows the radiocapitellar contact face, the sagittal shows the anterior-to-posterior extent and contour. And do not call Panner or OCD on a **normal CRITOE secondary ossification center**, which is fragmented-looking by nature.

**Pearl:** Panner is the whole young capitellum (~5–10 yr) that heals; OCD is the focal teenage capitellum (~12–17 yr) that can fragment and shed a loose body — age plus focality plus the presence of a fragment/cyst separates them.

#### Topic 2: OCD stability criteria: the fluid-undercutting line, cysts, and cartilage breach

### OCD Stability Criteria — the Interface Is Everything

Once you have called a capitellar OCD, the question that changes management is **stable vs unstable**: a stable lesion is rested and followed; an unstable lesion stops throwing and goes to surgery (fixation, microfracture, fragment removal ± grafting). Stability is judged **at the fragment–bone interface**, read on **coronal + sagittal**.

**Unstable — ANY of:**
- A **T2 fluid-bright line completely undercutting the fragment**, matching joint-fluid signal (the most reliable adult criterion)
- A **sizeable or multiple subchondral cyst(s)** deep to the fragment
- A **high-signal cartilage cleft reaching the fragment** (delamination)
- A **displaced fragment or a loose body**

**Stable:** no surrounding fluid-bright signal, intact overlying cartilage, an in-situ fragment, and a low-signal demarcating margin.

| Sign | What it means |
|---|---|
| Fluid line fully undercutting fragment | **Unstable** |
| Sizeable/multiple subchondral cyst | **Unstable** |
| Cartilage cleft to the fragment | **Unstable** (delamination) |
| Displaced fragment / loose body | **Unstable** |
| **Surrounding marrow edema alone** | **NOT a stability sign** (stable and unstable both have it) |
| **Isolated high-T2 line, no cyst/breach** | **INDETERMINATE** (may be healing granulation) |

**Hedges to teach honestly:** the **5 mm cyst** figure is **De Smet's KNEE-derived criterion** (Dipaola is a separate staging system), **not validated for the capitellum** — say "sizeable or multiple cysts," not a hard cutoff. And the often-quoted **~0.92 sensitivity / 0.85 specificity** for MRI stability criteria comes from an **OCD-wide (predominantly knee) meta-analysis extrapolated to the elbow** — cite it that way or teach the signs qualitatively. When stability is indeterminate and will change the operation, **MR or CT arthrography** resolves it.

**Pearl:** OCD instability lives at the interface — a fluid line undercutting the fragment, a sizeable or multiple cyst, a cartilage breach, or a displaced fragment; surrounding edema alone is NOT instability, and an isolated high-T2 line is indeterminate.

#### Topic 3: The thrower's stress lesions: Little League elbow, olecranon stress, and valgus extension overload

### The Thrower's Osseous Stress Lesions

Repetitive valgus loads the medial column in tension and the posterior column in extension. Three osseous stress patterns recur, and each is graded by the **presence or absence of a discrete fracture line**.

**Little League elbow (medial epicondyle apophysitis/avulsion)** — in the skeletally immature thrower, repetitive valgus **traction** injures the medial epicondyle apophysis: a **widened, fluid-bright physis with adjacent marrow edema**. Frank avulsion needs the exact displacement reported. **Operative triggers in a throwing athlete: displacement ≥ 5 mm, an intra-articular/incarcerated fragment, or valgus instability** — report the millimeters and whether the fragment is intra-articular. (The mm cutoff is debated in the general population; ≥ 5 mm is the widely taught throwing-athlete trigger, and incarceration or instability mandates surgery regardless of mm.)

**Olecranon stress** — a discrete **low-signal fracture LINE** (classically posteromedial/mid-olecranon) plus marrow edema = **stress fracture** (longer rest ± fixation, especially if widening/displaced); **edema WITHOUT a line = lower-grade stress reaction**. The **olecranon apophysis is among the last to fuse** — roughly **mid-to-late teens (~15–17 yr, later in throwers)** — so a persistently widened, fluid-bright olecranon physis is a physeal stress injury; **compare to the contralateral side** rather than trusting a hard fusion age.

**Valgus extension overload (VEO)** — the chronic end of the chain: UCL insufficiency lets the **posteromedial olecranon** impinge in the olecranon fossa, producing a **posteromedial olecranon osteophyte** and a posterior-compartment loose body. The osteophyte causes the catching, but the **underlying UCL must be assessed** — debriding the osteophyte without addressing valgus laxity can unmask instability.

| Lesion | Imaging | Management hinge |
|---|---|---|
| Little League elbow | Widened, bright physis + edema | ≥ 5 mm / intra-articular / unstable → surgery |
| Olecranon stress | Line = fracture; edema only = reaction | Line → longer rest ± fixation |
| VEO | Posteromedial osteophyte + loose body | Assess the UCL before debriding |

**Pearl:** Edema is the smoke — find the fire: a discrete low-signal line makes an olecranon stress FRACTURE (rest ± fixation) versus edema-only stress reaction, and a posteromedial olecranon osteophyte should always send you back to interrogate the UCL.

#### Topic 4: CRITOE, the radiocapitellar line, and the occult radial-head fracture (fat-pad sign)

### CRITOE, the Radiocapitellar Line, and the Occult Radial-Head Fracture

Three habits keep the bone read out of trouble: know the ossification order, check radial alignment, and respect the fat-pad sign.

**CRITOE** — the elbow's ossification sequence (approximate ages): **C**apitellum ~1, **R**adial head ~3, **I**nternal/medial epicondyle ~5, **T**rochlea ~7, **O**lecranon ~9, **E**xternal/lateral epicondyle ~11 — but these ages are approximate mnemonics with wide normal and sex-based variation, so trust the ORDER (and compare to the contralateral side), not the exact ages. The load-bearing rule: **the trochlea NEVER ossifies before the medial epicondyle.** If you 'see' the trochlea but **not** the medial epicondyle, the medial epicondyle has been **avulsed** — often into the joint.

**Radiocapitellar line** — a line through the **central long axis of the radial neck must bisect the capitellum on EVERY projection and plane**. Failure = radial head subluxation/dislocation, including an **occult Monteggia**. Repetitive valgus also drives radiocapitellar (lateral-compartment) osteochondral overload — the lateral partner of medial UCL injury.

**Occult radial-head fracture / fat-pad sign** — any **visible (displaced) POSTERIOR fat pad** in an adult after trauma means **hemarthrosis = occult intra-articular fracture** (radial head most common in adults; supracondylar in children) until proven otherwise. The anterior fat pad is normally seen; elevation is the **sail sign**. MRI shows a **low-signal line + fat-suppressed-bright marrow edema** and distinguishes a true fracture from a **bone bruise (edema only)**.

| Mason | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Non-/minimally displaced |
| II | Displaced (commonly > 2 mm) partial articular |
| III | Comminuted whole-head |

**Surgical trigger** is a **mechanical BLOCK to forearm rotation**, an articular fragment displaced enough to impede motion, or **comminution (Mason III)** — **NOT the 2 mm number alone**; many Mason II without a block are managed nonoperatively.

**Pearl:** A displaced posterior fat pad after adult elbow trauma is a fracture until disproven — radial head first — and the radial-head surgical trigger is a mechanical block to forearm rotation, not the 2 mm displacement by itself.

#### Topic 5: Loose bodies, primary OA, and the high-stakes pediatric misses

### Loose Bodies, Primary OA, and the High-Stakes Pediatric Misses

**Loose bodies** migrate to **dependent recesses** — the **coronoid fossa, olecranon fossa, and radiocapitellar recess** — and are best seen **against bright joint fluid on sagittal fluid-sensitive images**. **GRE blooms ossified bodies**, and **CT/CT arthrography is most sensitive** for small ossified bodies and for **counting them pre-operatively**. Always sweep the fossae in any locking/catching history, and check the **radiocapitellar plica (posterolateral synovial fold, pathologic when thickened beyond ~3 mm)** as a mimic.

**Don't ascribe every loose body to a throwing lesion.** **Primary elbow OA** — coronoid- and olecranon-tip osteophytes, fossa osteophytes, and capitellar/radial-head chondral loss — is the **most common source of loose bodies and mechanical symptoms in the non-thrower/laborer**.

**The high-stakes pediatric misses:**

| Miss | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Incarcerated medial epicondyle avulsion** | After a transient dislocation that **spontaneously reduced**, the avulsed medial epicondyle can be **trapped intra-articularly** — use CRITOE (trochlea visible without medial epicondyle → it's avulsed, often into the joint) |
| **Lateral condyle fracture (Salter-Harris IV)** | The elbow fracture **most prone to non-union/malunion**; often the subtle **'fat pad without an obvious line'** that needs MRI/follow-up, not reassurance |

Both pediatric lesions are the kind that look unimpressive on the first pass and declare themselves later — which is exactly why bones and marrow are **search-pattern step 2**, read while attention is freshest.

**Pearl:** Most non-thrower loose bodies come from primary elbow OA, not a throwing lesion — and in a child, a 'fat pad without a line' is a lateral condyle (Salter-Harris IV) fracture or an incarcerated medial epicondyle until proven otherwise.

#### Topic 1: UCL anatomy: anterior bundle from medial epicondyle to sublime tubercle as the primary valgus restraint

### The Anterior Bundle: One Band Does the Work

The **ulnar collateral ligament (UCL) complex** has three parts, but for the thrower only one matters. The **anterior bundle** is the **primary valgus restraint** across the **functional throwing arc (~20–120°)**, running from the **anteroinferior medial epicondyle** to the **sublime tubercle of the medial coronoid**. This is the band reconstructed in **Tommy John surgery**.

The other two contribute little to valgus restraint:
- **Posterior bundle** — forms the floor of the **cubital tunnel**; relevant to the ulnar nerve, not to valgus stability.
- **Transverse (Cooper) ligament** — negligible mechanical role.

**How to read it:** the normal anterior bundle is a **thin, taut, uniformly low-signal band** to the sublime tubercle, best on **coronal (or coronal-oblique) fluid-sensitive fat-sat** prescribed *along the band*. The common **flexor-pronator origin** drapes directly over it off the medial epicondyle, so trace the deep low-signal ligament separately from the overlying tendon.

| UCL component | Origin → insertion | Valgus role |
|---|---|---|
| **Anterior bundle** | Anteroinferior medial epicondyle → sublime tubercle | **Primary restraint (the Tommy John band)** |
| Posterior bundle | Medial epicondyle → olecranon (cubital tunnel floor) | Minimal |
| Transverse/Cooper | Coronoid → olecranon | Negligible |

**Course-image note:** the normal elbow workstation includes **coronal T2-FS, axial T2-FS, and sagittal IR/STIR** non-arthrographic stacks. Coronal remains the plane to read the anterior bundle and the T-sign conceptually, with the caveat below that MR arthrography is more sensitive for subtle undersurface tears.

**Pearl:** When a thrower presents with medial pain, the only valgus-relevant ligament is the anterior bundle from the medial epicondyle to the sublime tubercle — read it on coronal fluid-sensitive images prescribed along the band, and do not let the overlying flexor-pronator tendon distract you from the deep ligament.

#### Topic 2: Normal distal footprint and recess vs the abnormal undercutting threshold (beyond the cartilage edge)

### Normal Footprint, Normal Recess, and the Abnormal Threshold

The single most over-called finding here is a **normal distal synovial recess** mistaken for an undersurface tear. The distal footprint normally inserts as a **thin, taut, uniformly low-signal band on the sublime tubercle**, and a **small synovial recess can exist** right at that attachment.

The key is *where the fluid stops*:

| Finding | What you see | Call it |
|---|---|---|
| **Normal recess** | A sliver of fluid undercutting the **most distal fibers by a couple of mm**, NOT past the cartilage edge | Normal variant |
| **Partial undersurface tear (T-sign)** | Fluid/contrast tracking distal to the sublime tubercle **beyond the edge of the articular cartilage** | Abnormal |

The **abnormal threshold is fluid/contrast tracking distal to the sublime-tubercle margin BEYOND the edge of the articular cartilage** — not 'any undercutting at all.' A couple of millimeters undercutting the most distal fibers is within normal limits; the diagnosis turns on the fluid crossing the **articular-cartilage edge**.

**Why it matters:** over-calling a normal recess as a partial tear can push a thrower toward an injection or a surgical conversation he does not need; under-calling a true undersurface tear ends a season. Anchor every distal UCL call to the cartilage-edge landmark, and remember **MR arthrography is more sensitive** than the non-contrast study you are reading.

**Pearl:** The abnormal line for the distal UCL is the articular-cartilage edge: a couple of millimeters of fluid undercutting the most distal fibers is a normal recess, but contrast or fluid tracking BEYOND the cartilage margin along the sublime tubercle is the undersurface tear.

#### Topic 3: The T-sign: partial undersurface (articular-sided) distal tear and why MR arthrography is most sensitive

### The T-Sign: The Classic Throwing Injury

The **T-sign** is the signature of a **partial-thickness undersurface (articular-sided) DISTAL tear** — the classic throwing injury that is routinely **undercalled on non-contrast MRI**.

**What forms the 'T':** on coronal fluid-sensitive MRI or MR arthrography, **fluid/contrast tracks medially along the sublime tubercle UNDER the distal UCL attachment**, beyond the articular-cartilage edge. This medial limb of fluid meets the vertical **joint-fluid column** at a right angle, producing an **inverted / right-angle 'T'** — while the **ligament remains attached more proximally**. The deep fibers are stripped off the bone; the superficial fibers still hold.

| Feature | T-sign (undersurface partial) | Full-thickness tear |
|---|---|---|
| Fluid pattern | Medial limb undercutting distal fibers, beyond cartilage edge | Fluid-bright signal spanning the **entire** thickness |
| Proximal fibers | **Still attached** | Discontinuous / gap / retraction |
| Extravasation | Contained (articular-sided) | **Medial extravasation through the defect** |

**The arthrography caveat:** because the deep surface tear may not fill with native joint fluid on a non-contrast study, **MR arthrography is the most sensitive study** for the T-sign — intra-articular contrast actively undercuts the partial tear. On the coronal T2-FS stack in this normal workstation, teach the T-sign conceptually; if the question is surgically decisive and the non-contrast study is equivocal, **add MR arthrography**.

**Pearl:** The T-sign is fluid undercutting the distal UCL beyond the cartilage edge while the proximal fibers stay attached — it is the under-recognized, season-ending undersurface tear of the throwing elbow, and MR arthrography is the most sensitive study to confirm it when non-contrast imaging is equivocal.

#### Topic 4: Grading and locating: proximal/mid/distal, partial vs full-thickness, acute vs chronic

### Grade and Locate: The Report That Drives the Decision

The surgeon needs three things from your read: **surface, location, and chronicity.** Give all three explicitly.

**Surface / grade:**
- **Sprain / low-grade** — periligamentous or intrasubstance edema, fibers grossly intact, no surface-reaching defect, no T-sign.
- **Partial undersurface tear** — the T-sign; articular-sided, proximal fibers attached.
- **Full-thickness** — **fluid-bright signal spanning the entire thickness with a gap/retraction, or medial extravasation through the defect.**

A **<50% / >50% partial-thickness call is a useful descriptive heuristic, not a formally validated or named UCL grading scale.** Do not imply a validated cutoff drives the decision by itself.

**Location:** name it — **proximal (humeral) / midsubstance / distal (sublime tubercle).** This is not cosmetic: **distal (sublime-tubercle) tears are clinically important, more often occult, and do worse nonoperatively**, which is exactly why naming the location matters. But distal-vs-proximal **predominance is genuinely not uniform in the literature** — teach the distal tear as a recognized, prognostically worse, under-recognized pattern, **not** as the more frequent lesion. **Proximal partials** are the ones favored for nonoperative/PRP management.

**Chronicity:** a **thickened, heterogeneous, or ossified** ligament reads as **chronic**; clean acute fiber disruption reads as acute. The repair-vs-reconstruction conversation cares about **avulsion off bone (good tissue) vs a shredded chronic midsubstance.**

| Axis | Acute / favorable | Chronic / unfavorable |
|---|---|---|
| Surface | Clean partial or avulsion | Diffuse attenuation |
| Location | Proximal (better nonop/PRP) | Distal (worse nonop) |
| Tissue | Avulsion off bone | Thickened/heterogeneous/ossified |

**Pearl:** Always report surface, location, and chronicity: a <50%/>50% split is a descriptive heuristic and not a validated scale, distal/full-thickness tears do worse nonoperatively, and an 'avulsion off bone vs shredded midsubstance' distinction is exactly what the surgeon needs to choose repair over reconstruction.

#### Topic 5: The medial valgus triad (UCL + flexor-pronator + ulnar nerve) and the repair-vs-reconstruction decision

### The Medial Triad and the Repair-vs-Reconstruction Conversation

Medial-sided valgus overload **travels as a triad** — read all three on every thrower:

| Component | What to document |
|---|---|
| **UCL anterior bundle** | Surface, location, chronicity (above) |
| **Flexor-pronator (common flexor) origin** | Strain vs tendinosis off the medial epicondyle, overlying the UCL |
| **Ulnar nerve** | Neuritis (caliber + T2 signal) and **flexion subluxation** in the cubital tunnel |

A common incidental bony clue to chronic valgus overload is the **sublime-tubercle traction spur / ulnar traction marrow change**.

**Management is driven by the lesion PLUS the athlete's demand, not the MRI alone:**
- **Most partial/low-grade tears in throwers are managed nonoperatively first** — rest, flexor-pronator and kinetic-chain rehab, structured return-to-throwing; **PRP is increasingly used, especially for proximal partials.**
- **Reconstruction (Tommy John, autograft)** is the conversation for **full-thickness tears in throwers wanting high-level return**, **high-grade/symptomatic partials that fail ~3–6 months of rehab**, or **valgus instability discordant with demand.**
- **Repair ± internal brace** has re-emerged for **acute avulsion-type tears with good tissue**, particularly younger/lower-demand or earlier-return athletes — which is why the **avulsion-vs-shredded-midsubstance** distinction matters.

**Two hedges to honor:** the failed-conservative interval, the repair-vs-reconstruction boundary, and PRP efficacy are all **evolving — present decision DRIVERS, not a rigid cutoff.** And **ulnar nerve transposition with reconstruction is selective**, done for preoperative ulnar symptoms or instability, not routinely.

**The number MRI cannot give:** **dynamic valgus-stress ultrasound** quantifies side-to-side **medial ulnohumeral gapping** — a side-to-side difference of roughly **>1 mm (commonly cited ~1.5–2 mm)** is the functional instability threshold. For counseling, typical return is **~12–15 months after reconstruction vs ~6–9 months after repair + internal brace**, and prevention rests on **pitch-count/workload limits and avoiding breaking balls before skeletal maturity.**

**Pearl:** Read the UCL, the flexor-pronator origin, and the ulnar nerve as one valgus triad, then frame management by lesion plus demand — nonoperative/PRP for most partials, reconstruction for full-thickness or rehab-failed high-demand throwers, and repair ± internal brace for acute avulsions with good tissue — while remembering that dynamic valgus-stress ultrasound, not MRI, supplies the side-to-side gapping number.

#### Topic 1: LCL complex anatomy and the LUCL as the principal restraint to PLRI

### The LCL Complex and the LUCL as the PLRI Restraint

The **lateral collateral ligament (LCL) complex** is read on coronal and axial fluid-sensitive fat-sat images. It has four named components:

| Component | Course | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Radial collateral ligament (RCL)** | Lateral epicondyle → blends into the **annular ligament** | Lateral stabilizer; deep to the common extensor |
| **Lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL)** | Lateral epicondyle → arcs **posterior to the radial head** → **supinator crest** of the ulna | **Principal restraint to PLRI** |
| **Annular ligament** | Encircles the radial neck (best on **axial**) | Stabilizes the radial head |
| **Accessory LCL** | Supinator crest to annular ligament | Supports the annular ligament |

The **LUCL is the structure that matters most** here. It originates at the lateral epicondyle, sweeps **posterior to the radial head**, and inserts on the **supinator crest** — and it is the principal restraint to **posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI)**.

**Trace its posterolateral arc across consecutive coronal AND axial images.** Partial-volume averaging is the single most common reason the LUCL is missed — it is an oblique, curving structure, and one plane is not enough. A normal LUCL is a thin, continuous, uniformly low-signal band to the supinator crest.

**The anatomic trap:** the **LUCL/RCL origin lies deep to the common extensor tendon**. A deep undersurface tear there hides beneath what looks like simple lateral epicondylitis — which is exactly why the next topic exists.

**Pearl:** The LUCL runs lateral epicondyle → posterior to the radial head → supinator crest and is the key PLRI restraint — trace it on BOTH coronal and axial images, because partial-volume averaging on a single plane is the main reason it is missed.

#### Topic 2: PLRI mechanism and the deep undersurface tear hiding beneath refractory 'tennis elbow'

### PLRI and the Deep Tear Hiding Beneath Refractory 'Tennis Elbow'

**Posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI)** is **LUCL incompetence** that allows posterolateral subluxation of the radius and ulna off the capitellum — the lesion behind the clinical **lateral pivot-shift apprehension**. It is the **most common chronic/recurrent elbow instability** and follows:

- **Dislocation or subluxation** (the LUCL is usually the primary lesion — see the O'Driscoll circle)
- **Iatrogenic injury** from an **aggressive lateral epicondylitis release** or **radial-head surgery**

**Why this is a satisfaction-of-search trap:** the **LUCL/RCL origin sits deep to the common extensor tendon**. A deep undersurface tear at that origin can hide beneath what reads as routine lateral epicondylitis. This is **the lesion that converts refractory 'tennis elbow' into instability** — and it is a classic complication of prior **lateral corticosteroid injection or surgical release**.

**What separates the surgical lesion from over-call:**

| Finding | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Thickened, T2-bright common extensor, **intact deep fibers** | Tendinosis / partial CET tear → conservative |
| **Fluid-bright gap** at the origin that **undercuts/communicates with the radiocapitellar joint AND involves the LUCL** | Deep tear implying **PLRI → surgical** |
| Reactive lateral-epicondyle marrow edema, **thin RCL signal alone** | **Normal/reactive — do NOT call an LCL tear** |

**Always trace the LUCL to the supinator crest before signing off any high-grade lateral epicondylitis.** The companion ligament is where the surgery hides.

**Pearl:** A deep common-extensor tear with a fluid-bright gap that undercuts the radiocapitellar joint and involves the LUCL is PLRI, not tennis elbow — it is a known complication of prior lateral injection/release and turns a debridement into a ligament reconstruction.

#### Topic 3: O'Driscoll circle of dislocation, LCL grading vocabulary, and secondary PLRI signs

### O'Driscoll Circle, LCL Grading Vocabulary, and Secondary PLRI Signs

**The O'Driscoll circle of dislocation** explains the sequence of soft-tissue failure: disruption proceeds **lateral-to-medial** — **LCL/LUCL first → anterior and posterior capsule → finally the MCL.** Two consequences follow directly:

1. The **LCL is the primary lesion in most simple dislocations.**
2. Valgus stability is often **preserved**, because the MCL fails last.

So: **comment explicitly on LUCL integrity in every post-dislocation and post-lateral-surgery elbow.**

**Grading vocabulary** mirrors the UCL — there is **no standardized single MRI measurement** for an LUCL tear, so call it on **continuity, surface, and radiocapitellar alignment** plus the clinical pivot-shift, not a millimeter cutoff:

| Grade | Appearance |
|---|---|
| **Intact** | Thin, continuous, uniformly low-signal band to the supinator crest |
| **Sprain** | Thickening/edema, **fibers intact** |
| **Partial** | Surface-reaching fluid **not spanning** the full thickness |
| **Full-thickness** | Discontinuity/avulsion, usually **proximal/epicondylar** |

**Secondary PLRI signs** (look for these when the ligament itself is equivocal):
- **Posterolateral radial subluxation**
- **Radiocapitellar incongruity**
- **Lateral capsular and deep common-extensor tear**

Because there is no number to lean on, the **combination** of a discontinuous LUCL, radiocapitellar malalignment, and the clinical pivot-shift is what makes the call.

**Pearl:** Dislocation tears the elbow lateral-to-medial (O'Driscoll: LUCL → capsule → MCL last), so the LCL is the primary lesion in most simple dislocations and valgus is often spared — comment on LUCL integrity in every post-dislocation elbow.

#### Topic 4: Anteromedial coronoid facet fracture and varus posteromedial rotatory instability (VPMRI)

### Anteromedial Coronoid Facet Fracture and VPMRI

**Varus posteromedial rotatory instability (VPMRI)** is the **third, distinct instability mechanism** — separate from valgus instability and from PLRI. Its imaging signature is an **anteromedial coronoid facet fracture + LUCL injury**.

The critical teaching point: the **anteromedial facet fragment looks small on radiographs but is highly unstable and surgical.** Untreated VPMRI drives **early arthrosis**. This is a classic **don't-miss** MRI/CT call — **small does not mean stable at the coronoid.**

**Two coronoid classifications — use them for different things:**

| System | Classifies | Scheme |
|---|---|---|
| **O'Driscoll** | The **anteromedial facet** (the VPMRI lesion) | Anteromedial-facet subtypes |
| **Regan-Morrey** | Coronoid **height** | I = tip avulsion · II = ≤50% · III = >50% of coronoid height |

The coronoid is the **keystone of terrible-triad reconstruction**, which is why height matters surgically (Regan-Morrey), while the **anteromedial facet** specifically flags the **varus posteromedial mechanism** (O'Driscoll).

**Practical read:** in any post-trauma elbow, sweep the coronoid for **both** a height loss and an anteromedial-facet fragment. **CT maps the osseous fragment best** and is often added for surgical planning.

**Pearl:** An anteromedial coronoid facet fracture is small on radiographs but means varus posteromedial rotatory instability — a distinct, surgical instability that drives early arthrosis if missed; classify the facet with O'Driscoll and coronoid height with Regan-Morrey.

#### Topic 5: Terrible triad, the Osborne-Cotterill lesion, heterotopic ossification, and the management hinge

### Terrible Triad, Osborne-Cotterill, HO, and the Management Hinge

**The terrible triad** = **elbow dislocation + radial head fracture + coronoid fracture.** It is recognizable on MRI/CT and is **surgical**, with the **coronoid and LCL as the reconstructive keystones**.

After any dislocation, run the post-traumatic circle and look for two more findings:

- **Osborne-Cotterill lesion** — a **posterolateral capitellar impaction fracture + posterolateral capsular avulsion**. It is the **lateral analog of a Hill-Sachs**, perpetuates **recurrent PLRI**, and is a clue to **occult instability**.
- **Post-traumatic heterotopic ossification (HO)** — a common, **function-limiting cause of the stiff elbow** that changes management (timing of excision, indomethacin/radiation prophylaxis).

**The management hinge:**

| Scenario | Disposition |
|---|---|
| Acute LUCL injury after dislocation, elbow **concentrically reduced and stable** | Often **nonoperative** |
| **Persistent/recurrent PLRI** | **LUCL repair or reconstruction** |
| **Iatrogenic LUCL injury** (lateral release / radial-head surgery) | Recognized cause of **acquired PLRI** → surgical |
| **Anteromedial coronoid facet fracture (VPMRI)** | **Surgical** |
| **Terrible triad** | **Surgical** — coronoid + LCL keystones |

So the disposition turns on **whether the joint stays concentrically reduced**: a stable, reduced elbow with an LUCL injury can be treated nonoperatively, whereas recurrent PLRI, VPMRI, and the terrible triad all go to surgery.

**Pearl:** In the post-dislocation elbow, a concentrically reduced and stable joint with an LUCL injury is often nonoperative, but recurrent PLRI, the anteromedial coronoid facet (VPMRI), and the terrible triad are all surgical — and look for the Osborne-Cotterill impaction and heterotopic ossification on every one.

#### Topic 1: Distal biceps anatomy: bifurcated radial-tuberosity footprint, lacertus fibrosus, and bicipitoradial bursa

### Distal Biceps Anatomy: Footprint, Lacertus, and Bursa

Three structures decide every distal biceps read. Find them before you grade anything.

- **Bifurcated radial-tuberosity footprint.** The distal biceps inserts on the radial (bicipital) tuberosity as **two limbs with distinct jobs**: the **short head** attaches **distal/anterior** and is the **flexion lever**; the **long head** attaches **proximal/posterior** and is the dominant **supination lever**. A tear can be partial along one limb of this footprint, which is why undersurface partial tears are easy to under-grade.
- **Lacertus fibrosus.** An aponeurotic expansion arising from the **medial musculotendinous junction** that blends into the **forearm flexor fascia**. It is the single most important structure for prognosis: intact, it **tethers the tendon and limits retraction** even when the main tendon is fully torn.
- **Bicipitoradial bursa.** Sits **between the tendon and the tuberosity**; a distended bursa is a **clue**, not a diagnosis, and a large one can compress the **PIN/radial nerve**.

| Limb | Footprint position | Primary action |
|---|---|---|
| **Short head** | Distal / anterior | Flexion |
| **Long head** | Proximal / posterior | Supination |

**Best images:** the distal biceps is read on **axial fluid-sensitive fat-sat** and, ideally, the dedicated **FABS view** (Flexion–ABduction–Supination, prone), which lays the footprint flat for partial-tear grading and retraction measurement. The normal elbow workstation includes an axial T2-FS stack for tendon orientation but not a dedicated FABS acquisition, so use this as an anatomy anchor and avoid making footprint-grading claims from the teaching stack alone.

**Pearl:** Before grading any distal biceps tear, locate the lacertus fibrosus — it is the structure that determines whether a complete tear retracts, and therefore how urgently it must reach a surgeon.

#### Topic 2: Distal biceps grading and the lacertus as the discriminator that drives repair urgency

### Distal Biceps Grading and the Lacertus Discriminator

Walk the ladder, then add the two modifiers that actually change management.

| Grade | What you see |
|---|---|
| **Tendinosis** | Tendon **thickening + intrasubstance signal**, no discontinuity |
| **Partial** | **Fluid-bright** defect with **some fibers still reaching the tuberosity** (non-retracted), usually undersurface at the footprint |
| **Complete** | **Full discontinuity** with a fluid/hematoma gap and a **balled-up, wavy stump** |

**The lacertus changes everything.** Report it on every complete tear:
- **Complete + intact lacertus** → the tendon **does not retract** (or only minimally) and can **mimic a partial tear** clinically and on imaging. This is the **commonly missed complete tear** — do not be reassured by a preserved exam.
- **Complete + disrupted lacertus** → the tendon **retracts proximally**, leaving an **empty tuberosity**.

**Always report three things:** partial-vs-complete, **lacertus intact-vs-torn**, and **retraction (where the stump sits, in cm)**.

**Surgical timing — a guideline, not a deadline.** A complete retracted tear is a **time-sensitive surgical lesion in active patients**: earlier anatomic repair is **technically easier**, and chronicity/retraction increasingly push toward a **graft** when the tendon will no longer reach the tuberosity (often only after many weeks to months, once it is irreducible). **Do not teach a hard 2–4-week / graft-after-6-week cutoff** — many primary repairs succeed out to roughly 4–6 weeks and beyond. **Partial tears and tendinosis are managed conservatively first.** And remember: **bicipitoradial bursitis is a clue** — scrutinize the adjacent insertion for a partial tear and do not mistake a distended bursa for a mass.

**Pearl:** Report partial-vs-complete, lacertus-intact-vs-torn, and retraction in centimeters on every distal biceps tear — a complete tear with an intact lacertus is the great mimic that hides behind a near-normal exam.

#### Topic 3: Triceps tendon: deep-fiber-first avulsion, the under-diagnosed rupture, and percent-width reporting

### Triceps Tendon: Deep-Fiber-First Avulsion and the Under-Diagnosed Rupture

The triceps inserts **broadly on the olecranon**. Rupture is uncommon but is the **most under-diagnosed major tendon avulsion at the elbow** — partly because of how it fails.

**The deep-fiber-first pattern (a triceps analog of the biceps lacertus):** the **deep central fibers tear first** while the **superficial expansion can stay intact**. The result is that a clinically **near-complete, weak elbow can look 'partial'** on the images. So:
- **Report the percentage of tendon width torn and the gap** — not just 'partial.'
- **Do not undercall a high-grade partial as 'tendinosis'** if the patient cannot extend against resistance.

| Grade | Findings | Disposition |
|---|---|---|
| **Tendinosis** | Thickening + signal, no gap | Conservative |
| **High-grade partial** | **>50% width** torn, or **any tear with extension weakness** | Repair |
| **Complete** | Full-thickness gap, retracted stump, ± olecranon flake | Repair |

**Risk factors to raise suspicion:** **anabolic steroids, fluoroquinolones, renal failure / hyperparathyroidism, and local steroid injection.** The classic tip-off on the lateral radiograph is an **avulsed olecranon flake** — see it, and go looking for the tendon gap.

**Pearl:** Report the percentage of triceps width torn, not just 'partial' — the deep central fibers fail first under an intact superficial expansion, so a near-complete avulsion can masquerade as tendinosis in a patient who cannot extend against resistance.

#### Topic 4: Lateral epicondylitis (ECRB) and tracing the LUCL — the PLRI escalation

### Lateral Epicondylitis (ECRB) and Tracing the LUCL

'Tennis elbow' is **angiofibroblastic TENDINOSIS, not inflammation**, of the **common extensor origin** — the **ECRB is the principal culprit** — off the **lateral epicondyle**. Grade it on the same ladder you use elsewhere:

| Grade | Appearance |
|---|---|
| **Tendinosis** | Thickening + **intermediate signal**, no surface-reaching fluid/gap |
| **Partial** | **Fluid-bright** signal reaching a surface or a focal discontinuity, **intact deep fibers** |
| **Full-thickness** | Complete **fluid-bright gap** at the origin |

**The management-changing step: ALWAYS trace the LUCL to the supinator crest.** The LUCL/RCL origin lies **deep to the common extensor tendon**, so a deep tear can extend into the ligament. A **deep/full-thickness common-extensor tear with a fluid-bright gap that undercuts or communicates with the radiocapitellar joint and involves the LUCL implies PLRI (posterolateral rotatory instability)** — this converts a routine extensor debridement into a **ligament repair/reconstruction**, and it is a **known complication of prior lateral steroid injection or surgical release**.

**Two cautions:**
- **Do not over-call** associated **reactive lateral-epicondyle marrow edema** or a **thin reactive RCL signal** as an LCL tear.
- **Corticosteroid injection gives only short-term benefit and may worsen long-term outcomes** — frame expectations accordingly.

**Pearl:** Never sign off a high-grade lateral epicondylitis without tracing the LUCL to the supinator crest: a deep extensor tear that undercuts the radiocapitellar joint and involves the LUCL means PLRI and turns a debridement into a ligament reconstruction.

#### Topic 5: Medial epicondylitis (common flexor-pronator) and the medial valgus triad

### Medial Epicondylitis and the Medial Valgus Triad

'Golfer's elbow' is **common flexor-pronator tendinosis** off the medial epicondyle — **less common than lateral epicondylitis** but graded on the **same tendinosis → partial → full-thickness ladder**. The common flexor-pronator tendon **overlies the UCL**, which is exactly why the medial read cannot stop at the tendon.

**Read the medial side as a triad** (the three structures that travel together and fail together, especially in throwers):

| Component | Trace to | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Common flexor-pronator** | Medial epicondyle origin | The epicondylitis itself |
| **UCL (anterior bundle)** | **Sublime tubercle** of the coronoid | A concomitant tear shifts a thrower toward **reconstruction** |
| **Ulnar nerve** | **Cubital tunnel**, immediately posterior | Co-existing neuritis changes symptoms and treatment |

**The key linkage:** medial epicondylitis and UCL injury **frequently coexist**, and a **concomitant UCL tear changes the conversation toward reconstruction in a thrower** — so trace the **anterior bundle of the UCL to the sublime tubercle** and interrogate the **ulnar nerve** on every medial-pain study.

**Strain framework (applies to both sides):** the universal grade **1/2/3** scheme covers flexor-pronator and extensor **muscle** strains — **grade 1** = feathery perifascial edema with intact architecture; **grade 2** = fiber disruption with focal fluid/hematoma; **grade 3** = complete discontinuity with retraction, most common at the **myotendinous junction**.

**Pearl:** Medial pain is a triad — trace the flexor-pronator origin, the anterior-bundle UCL to the sublime tubercle, and the ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel — because a UCL tear riding under the epicondylitis is what redirects a thrower toward reconstruction.

Common mistakes:
- **Mistake:** Calling mild ulnar nerve T2 hyperintensity at the cubital tunnel 'neuritis' on signal alone
  **Correction:** Mild intraneural T2 brightness occurs in asymptomatic elbows (the nerve angulates and is closely apposed at the cubital tunnel). Anchor the diagnosis to caliber enlargement at/just distal to the medial epicondyle PLUS target-muscle (FCU, ulnar FDP, ulnar intrinsic) denervation PLUS clinical correlation, comparing the nerve to the ipsilateral median nerve and the contralateral side as internal controls — not signal in isolation.
- **Mistake:** Labeling subacute muscle denervation edema as 'acute' and reading an AIN palsy as a mechanical compression that needs decompression
  **Correction:** Denervation has THREE phases: NO MRI change for the first ~2–4 weeks, then SUBACUTE T2/STIR muscle edema (potentially reversible with reinnervation), then CHRONIC T1 fatty atrophy — so the edema phase is subacute, not acute. And an AIN (pure-motor) palsy is frequently neuralgic amyotrophy (Parsonage–Turner), so surgery is usually NOT indicated; pivot to EMG and observation and search the history for a viral prodrome or brachial-plexopathy pattern.
- **Mistake:** Asserting supinator denervation as a constant feature of PIN syndrome, or over-reading a normal MRI in radial tunnel syndrome
  **Correction:** In PIN syndrome the supinator is variably involved and often SPARED because its motor branches frequently arise proximal to the arcade of Frohse; localize instead by extensor-compartment denervation (extensor digitorum, EDM, ECU, APL, EPB/EPL, EI). Radial tunnel syndrome is pain WITHOUT a motor deficit and often has a NORMAL MRI — say so, and report any mass (ganglion, lipoma, distended bicipitoradial bursa) at the arcade rather than manufacturing a finding.

#### Topic 1: General nerve-entrapment signs and the THREE-phase denervation timeline (no change → subacute edema → chronic fatty atrophy)

### General Nerve-Entrapment Signs & the Three-Phase Denervation Timeline

A peripheral nerve is read the same way wherever it is entrapped. Look for **intraneural T2 hyperintensity**, **focal caliber enlargement at or just proximal to the compression** (the nerve swells where it is squeezed), **loss of the normal fascicular architecture**, and an **identifiable structural cause** (ganglion, accessory muscle, osteophyte, mass). But the **highest-yield finding is the secondary sign: denervation of the target muscles** — the muscle pattern is what localizes the lesion.

**Denervation has THREE phases, not two** — this is the classic trap:

| Phase | Timing | MRI finding | Reversible? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acute (latent) | First **~2–4 weeks** | **NO MRI change** | — |
| **Subacute** | Weeks–months | **T2/STIR muscle edema** (diffuse, feathery) | **Yes, with reinnervation** |
| **Chronic** | Months+ | **T1 fatty atrophy** | No |

- Label the edema phase **"subacute," not "acute"** — there is no MRI muscle change in the first few days, so muscle edema means the process is already weeks old.
- **Name the affected muscles** explicitly; the constellation (e.g., isolated pronator quadratus vs. the whole extensor compartment vs. FCU + ulnar intrinsics) is what tells the clinician which nerve and which level.

**Management:** subacute (edema-phase) denervation is the window where decompression of a true mechanical lesion can still recover muscle; chronic T1 fatty atrophy signals an established, often irreversible lesion. Staging the denervation frames the urgency.

**Pearl:** Muscle denervation edema is SUBACUTE, never acute — there is no MRI change for the first ~2–4 weeks, so visible T2/STIR muscle edema already dates the process to weeks, and the edema is reversible while the later T1 fatty atrophy is not.

#### Topic 2: Ulnar nerve / cubital tunnel: the most common elbow neuropathy and the internal-control comparison technique

### Ulnar Nerve / Cubital Tunnel: the Internal-Control Technique

The **cubital tunnel** is the most common site of elbow neuropathy and the **second most common compression neuropathy overall** (after carpal tunnel). The ulnar nerve runs **between the medial epicondyle and the olecranon, beneath the cubital tunnel retinaculum (Osborne's)**, where it is superficial, angulated, and stretched in flexion.

**What to report (best on axial):**
- **Caliber enlargement and T2 hyperintensity at or just distal to the medial epicondyle.**
- **Denervation of the ulnar-innervated muscles: FCU, ulnar (medial) half of FDP, and the ulnar intrinsics** of the hand — the localizing secondary sign.
- A structural cause: **anconeus epitrochlearis**, ganglion, osteophyte, or the medial valgus-overload changes.

**The technique that fixes the absent CSA cutoff:** there is **no validated universal cross-sectional-area threshold**, and **mild T2 brightness can be normal**. Assess at the **medial-epicondyle level** and compare the nerve's caliber and signal to **two internal controls**:

| Comparator | Why |
|---|---|
| **Contralateral ulnar nerve** | Side-to-side asymmetry flags abnormal enlargement/signal |
| **Ipsilateral MEDIAN nerve** | A same-arm normal nerve calibrates 'how bright is too bright' |

Anchor the diagnosis to **caliber change + denervation + clinical correlation**, not signal alone. The ulnar nerve is the **third member of the medial valgus-overload triad** in throwers — with the UCL and the flexor-pronator origin — so on a thrower's medial-pain study, read all three.

**Management:** a structural, denervating cubital-tunnel lesion goes to decompression; isolated mild signal without caliber change or denervation does not.

**Pearl:** There is no universal CSA cutoff for the ulnar nerve — compare its caliber and signal to the ipsilateral median nerve and the contralateral side, and anchor the call to caliber change plus muscle denervation plus clinical correlation, because mild T2 brightness alone can be normal.

#### Topic 3: Ulnar nerve subluxation, anconeus epitrochlearis, and snapping triceps — findings that change the operation

### Ulnar Subluxation, Anconeus Epitrochlearis & Snapping Triceps

This is where the imaging **changes the operation** — in-situ decompression versus transposition versus addressing the triceps.

**Ulnar nerve subluxation/dislocation** — the nerve **rides anteriorly over the medial epicondyle in flexion**. Assess on **axial images in flexion** (a dynamic problem a static extended image can miss). Because the unstable nerve will re-subluxate over an in-situ decompression, **subluxation favors anterior transposition** over simple release.

**Anconeus epitrochlearis** — an **accessory muscle that replaces Osborne's retinaculum**, roofing the cubital tunnel. It both crowds the nerve (a space-occupying cause) and is a structural lesion the surgeon must address; recognize it as muscle (not a mass or ganglion) spanning the epicondyle-to-olecranon roof.

**Snapping triceps — the coexisting trap:** the **medial head of the triceps can subluxate over the medial epicondyle in flexion together with the ulnar nerve.** Recognizing it is management-changing: **transposing the nerve alone leaves the snapping triceps**, so the operation must also address the triceps. **Dynamic/flexion imaging is the key** — a static neutral study underdiagnoses both the nerve and the triceps subluxation.

| Finding (assess in FLEXION) | Why it changes management |
|---|---|
| Ulnar nerve over the epicondyle | Favors **transposition**, not in-situ release |
| Medial triceps head subluxing with the nerve | Must **address the triceps**, not just transpose |
| Anconeus epitrochlearis / ganglion / osteophyte | Identifies the **structural cause** to resect |

**Management:** flag dynamic subluxation, the accessory muscle, and any coexisting snapping triceps explicitly — each one alters the surgical plan.

**Pearl:** Ulnar nerve subluxation favors transposition over in-situ decompression, and the medial triceps head can subluxate WITH the nerve (snapping triceps) — a flexion/dynamic image is what reveals both, and missing the triceps means the operation fails.

#### Topic 4: Median/AIN: pure-motor AIN palsy, the Parsonage-Turner caveat, and pronator syndrome

### Median / AIN: Pure-Motor Palsy, the Parsonage-Turner Caveat & Pronator Syndrome

Two distinct median-nerve syndromes at the elbow — keep them separate, because one of them often shouldn't go to surgery.

**Anterior interosseous nerve (AIN, Kiloh–Nevin) — PURE MOTOR.** It denervates **flexor pollicis longus (FPL), FDP to the index/middle fingers, and pronator quadratus**, producing **weak pinch (an abnormal 'OK' sign) and NO sensory loss**. On MRI, look for the localizing pattern — **classically isolated pronator quadratus edema** (subacute) on axial fat-sat images.

**The caveat that stops a surgeon:** an AIN palsy is **frequently neuralgic amyotrophy (Parsonage–Turner)**, NOT a mechanical compression. So **surgery is usually NOT indicated**; the work-up pivots to **EMG and observation**, and the history search turns to a **viral/post-vaccination prodrome or a broader brachial-plexus pattern** (other muscles outside a single nerve territory). Don't reflexively route isolated PQ/FPL denervation to decompression.

**Pronator syndrome — MIXED sensory + motor.** This is **median-nerve compression** more proximally, at one of several sites:

| Compression site |
|---|
| Between the two heads of **pronator teres** |
| Under the **lacertus fibrosus** |
| At the **FDS (sublimis) arch** |
| **Ligament of Struthers** (watch for a bony **supracondylar process**) |

Unlike isolated AIN palsy, pronator syndrome has **sensory symptoms** (volar forearm/hand) plus motor findings; on MRI hunt the supracondylar process and any mass at these sites.

**Management:** isolated pure-motor AIN palsy → think Parsonage–Turner, EMG/observation; a demonstrable structural compressive cause → decompression.

**Pearl:** An isolated pure-motor AIN palsy (weak pinch, no sensory loss, classically isolated pronator-quadratus edema) is frequently Parsonage–Turner neuralgic amyotrophy — so it usually does NOT need surgery; pivot to EMG and observation rather than decompression.

#### Topic 5: Radial/PIN at the arcade of Frohse, radial tunnel syndrome with a normal MRI, and hunting the mass

### Radial / PIN at the Arcade of Frohse, Radial Tunnel Syndrome & the Mass

The radial nerve divides at the **radiocapitellar level** into the superficial (sensory) branch and the **posterior interosseous nerve (PIN)**, which enters the supinator. The PIN is **most commonly compressed at the arcade of Frohse** — the proximal, often **fibrous** edge of the supinator.

**PIN syndrome — MOTOR.** It produces a **digit drop** (failure to extend the MCPs) with **radially-deviated wrist extension** because **ECRL is spared** (its branch leaves before the supinator). Localize by **denervation of the extensor compartment**: extensor digitorum, EDM, ECU, APL, EPB/EPL, EI.

- **The supinator is variably involved and often SPARED** — its motor branches frequently arise **proximal to the arcade**. **Do NOT assert supinator denervation as a constant PIN feature.**

**Radial tunnel syndrome — PAIN, no motor deficit.** Lateral forearm pain **without weakness and often a NORMAL MRI.** Say so rather than over-read; the value of imaging here is to **report (or exclude) a mass** at the arcade — **ganglion, lipoma, or a distended bicipitoradial bursa**.

| Entity | Motor deficit | MRI |
|---|---|---|
| **PIN syndrome** | Yes (digit drop, ECRL spared) | Extensor-compartment denervation; supinator often spared |
| **Radial tunnel syndrome** | No | Often normal — hunt for a mass at the arcade |

**Always look for a mass/cyst/ganglion as the compressive cause — anywhere along any of these nerves.** A space-occupying lesion **changes management from splinting/therapy to surgical referral**, so it is the finding worth the extra pass.

**Pearl:** PIN syndrome localizes by extensor-compartment denervation with ECRL spared, but the supinator is often spared and must NOT be claimed as a constant sign; radial tunnel syndrome is pain with a normal MRI — in both, the management-changing job is to find or exclude a mass at the arcade of Frohse.

#### Topic 1: Occult fracture behind a normal radiograph: the fat-pad sign, radial head, and the lateral condyle in children

### Occult Fracture Behind a Normal Radiograph

The classic miss: a post-traumatic elbow, a radiograph read as **normal**, and a patient sent to rehab — when the radiograph already told you a fracture is there. The tell is the **fat pad**.

- The **anterior fat pad** is normally seen and can elevate into a **sail sign**; the **posterior fat pad sits hidden in the olecranon fossa** and is normally invisible.
- **Any visible (displaced) posterior fat pad in an adult after trauma = hemarthrosis from an occult intra-articular fracture until proven otherwise** — most commonly the **radial head**.

Who fractures what:

| Patient | First suspect | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Adult** | **Radial head** | Most common occult intra-articular fracture; check the radiocapitellar line for an associated subluxation (occult Monteggia) |
| **Child** | **Supracondylar**, then the **lateral condyle** | The lateral condyle is a **Salter-Harris IV that is prone to non-union** if missed |

**MRI's job:** show the **low-signal fracture line on T1 plus surrounding marrow edema**, and separate a true fracture from a simple bone bruise. Always run the **radiocapitellar line** — a line through the central long axis of the radial neck must bisect the capitellum on every projection; failure signals radial-head subluxation/dislocation.

**Mason framework (radial head):** I non-/minimally displaced, II displaced partial-articular (commonly >2 mm), III comminuted whole-head. Note the trap: the **surgical trigger is a mechanical block to forearm rotation or comminution (Mason III), NOT the 2 mm number alone** — many Mason II fractures without a block are managed nonoperatively.

**Pearl:** A visible posterior fat pad after elbow trauma is a fracture until proven otherwise — search the radial-head and (in children) lateral-condyle marrow on MRI even when the radiograph is called normal.

#### Topic 2: Surgically urgent soft-tissue avulsions: complete distal biceps (and the intact-lacertus mimic), triceps, full-thickness UCL, deep extensor/LUCL

### Surgically Urgent Soft-Tissue Avulsions

These are the soft-tissue injuries that convert a rehab plan into a same-week surgical conversation. Name each one in the impression — do not let it sink into a list.

**Complete distal biceps avulsion.** Look for an **empty radial tuberosity** with a **balled-up, retracted, wavy stump**; a complete tear loses roughly **40–50% of supination and ~30% of flexion strength**. The **great mimic**: a complete tear with an **INTACT lacertus fibrosus**, which tethers the tendon so it does **not retract** and the exam looks deceptively preserved — it is routinely undercalled as a partial tear. Report **partial-vs-complete, retraction in cm, and lacertus status**, and refer promptly while the tendon still reaches the tuberosity. **There is no hard repair deadline** — earlier anatomic repair is technically easier and many primary repairs succeed out to ~4–6 weeks and beyond; only chronic, irreducible, retracted tears that will not reach the tuberosity need a graft. Bicipitoradial bursitis is a **clue** to scrutinize the footprint, not a diagnosis. FABS positioning lays the footprint flat for grading.

**Complete triceps avulsion.** A full-thickness gap with a retracted stump, often with a small **olecranon flake**. The deep central fibers fail first, so a tear can **look partial despite near-complete extension weakness** — report the percentage of width torn and the gap. Complete rupture is surgical; high-grade partials (commonly **>50% width**, or **any tear with extension weakness against resistance**) are repaired.

**Full-thickness UCL in a thrower** and a **deep common-extensor tear involving the LUCL** are the ligamentous members of this list (developed in the next topic and in the medial/lateral modules). Each ends a season or changes an operation if missed.

**Pearl:** Do not be reassured by a preserved exam in a suspected distal-biceps rupture — an intact lacertus tethers a completely torn tendon so it neither retracts nor gaps; report lacertus integrity and retraction explicitly.

#### Topic 3: Bony instability you must name: the anteromedial coronoid facet (VPMRI) and the terrible triad

### Bony Instability You Must Name: The Anteromedial Coronoid Facet and the Terrible Triad

Two osseous patterns look small on radiographs but are inherently **unstable and surgical**. They are the bony don't-miss findings of the post-trauma elbow.

**Anteromedial coronoid facet fracture.** The **coronoid is the keystone of post-traumatic stability** (sublime tubercle medially, anteromedial facet laterally). A fracture of the **anteromedial facet** can look like a tiny rim fragment yet it implies **varus posteromedial rotatory instability (VPMRI)** — a distinct mechanism from PLRI — and, untreated, drives **early arthrosis**. Classify it: **O'Driscoll for the anteromedial facet**, **Regan–Morrey by height** (I tip avulsion, II ≤50%, III >50%). **CT maps the osseous fragments best.**

**The terrible triad** = **dislocation + radial head fracture + coronoid fracture**. It is unstable, and the **coronoid and the LCL are the reconstructive keystones** — name them. Remember the **O'Driscoll circle**: a dislocation disrupts soft tissue from **lateral to medial (LUCL → capsule → MCL)**, so the **LCL is the primary lesion in most simple dislocations** — comment on LUCL integrity in **every** post-dislocation elbow.

| Don't-miss bony finding | Implies | Route |
|---|---|---|
| **Anteromedial coronoid facet fracture** | VPMRI | Surgery (CT to map) |
| **Terrible triad** | Gross instability | Surgery — coronoid + LCL keystones |
| **Osborne–Cotterill lesion** | Prior dislocation, recurrent instability | Flag; correlate with PLRI |
| **Heterotopic ossification** | Stiff post-trauma elbow | Flag in any post-dislocation/stiff elbow |

On any post-dislocation study, hunt the **Osborne–Cotterill posterolateral capitellar impaction** and **post-traumatic heterotopic ossification**.

**Pearl:** A small anteromedial coronoid facet fragment is a surgical lesion — it implies varus posteromedial rotatory instability; classify it (O'Driscoll), get a CT to map it, and comment on LUCL integrity in every post-dislocation elbow.

#### Topic 4: Infection: septic bursa vs septic joint vs osteomyelitis, and why a warm bursa is aspirated, not just imaged

### Infection: Septic Bursa vs Septic Joint vs Osteomyelitis

The elbow is a common site for **olecranon bursitis**, and the single most important teaching point is a limitation, not a sign: **MRI cannot reliably exclude infection.**

- A **red, warm, fluctuant olecranon bursa** needs **ASPIRATION**, not just imaging. Do not let a normal-looking or 'reactive' MRI talk a clinician out of tapping a bursa that is clinically inflamed.
- **Aseptic bursitis** is caused by **gout, rheumatoid arthritis, and repetitive trauma** — the picture can overlap completely with septic bursitis on MRI.

When the deeper compartments are involved, the threshold to escalate drops fast:

| Process | Imaging clue | Don't-miss point |
|---|---|---|
| **Septic bursitis** | Distended bursa ± rim enhancement | MRI cannot exclude it — **aspirate the warm bursa** |
| **Septic joint** | **Joint effusion + aggressive periarticular edema + rim enhancement** | Escalate urgently; not a rehab problem |
| **Osteomyelitis** | Marrow edema/replacement with cortical involvement | Cannot be confidently separated from aggressive inflammation on signal alone |

**Joint effusion plus aggressive periarticular edema and rim-enhancement escalates urgently.** The role of MRI here is to **map extent and raise the alarm**, not to grant clearance — the diagnosis is made by **aspiration and the clinical picture**.

**Pearl:** A warm, fluctuant olecranon bursa is aspirated, not just imaged — MRI cannot exclude infection, and gout, RA, and repetitive trauma all mimic a septic bursa.

#### Topic 5: The marrow red flag: confluent low-T1 replacement vs the benign mimics that also drop T1

### The Marrow Red Flag: Confluent Low-T1 Replacement — and Its Benign Mimics

The highest-stakes marrow call is **infiltration/tumor**, and the anchor is **T1**, not the fluid-sensitive sequence.

**The red-flag pattern:** **confluent low-T1 marrow signal that drops BELOW skeletal muscle or disc and lacks interspersed fat.** This is **marrow replacement** — tumor or infiltration until proven otherwise — and it is most convincing when it keeps dangerous company:

- an associated **soft-tissue mass**,
- **cortical destruction**, or
- a **wide (ill-defined) transition zone**.

Do **not** bury a replacement pattern as 'edema' on the fat-sat images — edema is bright on fluid-sensitive sequences but, on its own, does not produce confluent sub-muscle T1 signal.

**The honest caveat — benign processes also lower T1.** Frame the heuristic carefully:

| Lowers T1 but usually benign | What gives it away |
|---|---|
| **Red-marrow reconversion** (athletes, smokers) | Signal stays **at or above** muscle, spares subchondral/epiphyseal fat, symmetric |
| **Fracture / stress-injury edema** | A discrete line or a clinical injury; geographic, not mass-like |
| **Osteomyelitis** | Clinical infection; cannot always be separated on signal alone |

The discriminator is **signal level relative to muscle/disc, loss of interspersed fat, and the company it keeps** (mass, cortical breach, wide transition zone) — not a bright fluid-sensitive signal alone. When the pattern is confluent, sub-muscle, fat-poor, and accompanied by a mass or cortical destruction, escalate for tissue characterization rather than reassuring the patient.

**Pearl:** Confluent low-T1 marrow that drops below muscle/disc and lacks interspersed fat is replacement until proven otherwise — but red-marrow reconversion, fracture, and osteomyelitis also lower T1, so escalate on the company it keeps (mass, cortical destruction, wide transition zone), not T1 alone.

Clinical question: thrower's medial elbow pain with a 'pop' at release and a positive moving-valgus test — UCL undersurface tear versus medial epicondylitis versus ulnar neuritis. BONES & MARROW: Marrow edema at the sublime tubercle of the medial coronoid with a small adjacent traction-type change, consistent with chronic valgus overload at the distal UCL attachment. No capitellar osteochondral lesion (no fluid-undercut fragment, no sizeable or multiple subchondral cysts, no displaced fragment); the radiocapitellar lateral-compartment surface is preserved. No posteromedial olecranon (valgus-extension-overload) osteophyte and no olecranon stress line. Medial epicondyle physis is closed (skeletally mature) — this is a ligament/tendon injury, not a Little League apophyseal avulsion.

LIGAMENTS (UCL — key region): The anterior bundle of the UCL is thickened with intrasubstance T2 hyperintensity, indicating a chronic-on-acute process. At the DISTAL (sublime-tubercle) attachment, intra-articular contrast/fluid undermines the deep fibers and tracks BEYOND the edge of the articular cartilage — the T-sign — while the more proximal/humeral fibers remain attached. This is a partial-thickness, articular-sided (undersurface) DISTAL tear, not a full-thickness tear (no fiber discontinuity spanning the whole thickness, no gap/retraction, no medial extravasation through a complete defect). Estimated involvement is greater than 50% of the undersurface fibers — reported as a descriptive heuristic, not a validated graded scale. The lateral ligament complex (LUCL to the supinator crest, RCL, annular ligament) is intact, with no posterolateral rotatory-instability signs.

TENDONS: The common flexor-pronator origin off the medial epicondyle shows tendinosis (thickening and intermediate signal) without a surface-reaching fluid gap or full-thickness tear. The common extensor origin, distal biceps (footprint and lacertus), and triceps insertion are intact.

NERVES: The ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel shows mild T2 hyperintensity without definite caliber enlargement; no flexion subluxation demonstrated on the available images, no anconeus epitrochlearis, and no FCU/ulnar-intrinsic denervation edema. Findings are at most low-grade ulnar neuritis — anchor any neuritis call to caliber change plus denervation plus clinical correlation, not signal alone. Median/AIN and radial/PIN territories are unremarkable.

JOINT: Small joint effusion/contrast distension as expected for an arthrogram. Radiocapitellar and ulnotrochlear cartilage preserved; no loose body in the coronoid, olecranon, or radiocapitellar recesses; no pathologic radiocapitellar plica.

Model report impression:

1. Partial-thickness UNDERSURFACE (articular-sided) DISTAL tear of the UCL anterior bundle at the sublime tubercle — the T-SIGN — with a thickened, intrasubstance-abnormal ligament (chronic-on-acute). This is the management-changing finding: a distal undersurface tear is prognostically worse, more often occult, and is the lesion missed on non-contrast MRI and the static valgus exam (at arthroscopy it is seen only with applied valgus stress).
2. Sublime-tubercle and adjacent ulnar marrow edema/traction change, consistent with chronic valgus overload.
3. Flexor-pronator origin tendinosis without a full-thickness tear.
4. Mild ulnar nerve T2 signal without caliber change, subluxation, or denervation — at most low-grade neuritis; correlate clinically.
5. The full valgus-overload triad is documented (UCL + flexor-pronator + ulnar nerve). Treatment is driven by the lesion (distal, undersurface, >50% by heuristic) PLUS the positive moving-valgus exam PLUS this collegiate pitcher's demand — NOT by MRI alone. Recommend sports-medicine/orthopedic referral for an operative-versus-nonoperative discussion — repair ± internal brace favors an ACUTE, good-quality avulsion-type tear, whereas this thickened, chronic-on-acute distal ligament in a high-demand pitcher leans toward reconstruction (Tommy John); concurrent ulnar nerve transposition is selective, not routine, and is not indicated by these low-grade nerve findings.

Teaching points:
- The T-sign IS the partial undersurface tear: intra-articular contrast/fluid undercuts the most distal UCL fibers at the sublime tubercle and tracks BEYOND the edge of the articular cartilage while the proximal fibers stay attached. The abnormal threshold is fluid extending past the cartilage margin — a couple of millimetres of undercutting can be a normal distal synovial recess. MR arthrography is the most sensitive study; this lesion is undercalled on non-contrast MRI and occult on the static valgus-stress exam — at arthroscopy it is detected only when valgus stress is applied (the arthroscopic valgus-stress test, Timmerman–Andrews), not on resting visual inspection.
- Always characterize four things: surface (undersurface/articular-sided vs full-thickness), location (proximal/mid/DISTAL), acute vs chronic, and a size estimate. The <50%/>50% partial call is a descriptive heuristic, NOT a validated graded UCL scale — do not imply a cutoff drives the decision by itself.
- Distal (sublime-tubercle) tears are a recognized, prognostically WORSE, and more often occult pattern, and they do worse nonoperatively — which is why naming the location matters. But distal-versus-proximal predominance is genuinely NOT uniform in the literature; teach distal tears as an important under-recognized pattern, not as the more common lesion (several series report proximal/humeral-sided partials as at least as frequent).
- Management is driven by the lesion + the moving-valgus exam + the athlete's demand, not the MRI alone. Repair ± internal brace has re-emerged for acute avulsion-type tears with good tissue (often younger/earlier-return athletes), while reconstruction (Tommy John) is the conversation for full-thickness tears or high-grade/symptomatic partials in throwers wanting high-level return — so 'avulsion off bone vs shredded midsubstance' is a distinction the surgeon wants.
- Every thrower's medial-pain read is the valgus triad: UCL + flexor-pronator origin + ulnar nerve — they injure together. Document ulnar neuritis/subluxation, but concurrent ulnar nerve transposition with UCL surgery is SELECTIVE (for preoperative ulnar symptoms or instability), not routine; anchor any neuritis call to caliber change + denervation + clinical correlation, not T2 signal alone.

### Capitellar Osteochondritis Dissecans — Stable vs Unstable

ID: `elbow-capitellar-ocd` · Difficulty: intermediate · Tier: core · Resident visible: true

Clinical scenario: A 13-year-old competitive gymnast (also pitches Little League) presents with several months of progressive lateral elbow pain, intermittent catching, and a fixed 15-degree loss of terminal extension (flexion contracture). Pain worsens with weight-bearing on the arms and with throwing; radiographs show a focal anterolateral capitellar lucency, prompting MRI to characterize the lesion and judge its stability.

Key diagnoses:
- Capitellar osteochondritis dissecans (OCD) of the adolescent thrower/gymnast
- Stability assessment: fluid undercutting / subchondral cyst(s) / cartilage breach / displaced fragment as the management hinge
- Panner disease (the key age-based differential — excluded here)
- Radiocapitellar (lateral compartment) valgus-overload osteochondral injury
Search pattern findings:
- **Step 1: Verify Protocol & Clinical Question**
  - Confirm fluid-sensitive (PD/T2 FS) and T1 sequences with BOTH coronal and sagittal coverage of the capitellum — OCD is read on coronal + sagittal together
  - State the exact question: capitellar OCD vs Panner, and (if OCD) stable vs unstable, since that drives rest vs surgical referral
  - Lateral-pain, mechanically-catching adolescent gymnast/thrower with a capitellar lucency on radiographs — map the search to the radiocapitellar (OCD) face
- **Step 2: Bones, Marrow & Osteochondral**
  - FOCAL anterolateral capitellar osteochondral lesion (OCD), NOT diffuse whole-nucleus involvement — excludes Panner by focality and age
  - UNSTABLE interface: a T2 fluid-bright line (matching joint fluid) completely undercutting the fragment + a sizeable/multiple subchondral cyst(s) + a cartilage cleft reaching the fragment — the management-changing findings, read on coronal + sagittal
  - Surrounding marrow edema is present but explicitly NOT used as a stability sign; no medial epicondyle apophyseal stress lesion; radial head/trochlea/olecranon normal
- **Step 3: Medial Ligament — UCL & Valgus Overload**
  - Anterior-bundle UCL intact from medial epicondyle to sublime tubercle — NO T-sign (no fluid beyond the articular-cartilage edge) (pertinent negative in a thrower)
  - No sublime-tubercle traction spur or marrow edema of chronic valgus overload
  - Flexor-pronator origin and ulnar nerve (the medial triad) are addressed and normal
- **Step 4: Lateral Ligament — LCL Complex, PLRI & Coronoid**
  - LUCL (epicondyle → posterior to radial head → supinator crest), RCL, and annular ligament intact — no posterolateral rotatory instability sign
  - No coronoid anteromedial-facet fracture and no terrible-triad pattern (no post-traumatic instability here)
  - Radiocapitellar alignment maintained; the lesion is osteochondral, not ligamentous
- **Step 5: Tendons — Biceps, Triceps & Epicondyle Origins**
  - Common extensor (ECRB) origin normal — no lateral epicondylitis to account for the lateral pain (the OCD is the source)
  - Common flexor-pronator origin normal
  - Distal biceps and triceps insertions intact
- **Step 6: Nerves — Ulnar, Median / AIN, Radial / PIN**
  - Ulnar nerve normal in caliber/signal vs the median-nerve internal control and contralateral side; no flexion subluxation
  - No median/AIN or radial/PIN denervation (no target-muscle edema or fatty atrophy)
  - No mass or ganglion as an entrapment cause
- **Step 7: Cartilage, Joint, Capsule, Bursa & Synovium**
  - Overlying radiocapitellar cartilage shows a high-signal cleft REACHING the fragment (a cartilage breach — an instability criterion); ulnotrochlear cartilage preserved
  - Small effusion; dependent recesses (coronoid/olecranon fossae, radiocapitellar recess) swept and CLEAR of a separate loose body
  - No pathologically thickened radiocapitellar plica and no over-call of the normal capitellar pseudodefect as the lesion
- **Step 8: Masses, Management Review & Red Flags**
  - No marrow-replacement, cortical-destruction, or soft-tissue-mass red flags — this is osteochondral, not infection/tumor
  - Confirm the single management-changing finding: an UNSTABLE capitellar OCD (fluid undercutting + cyst + cartilage breach) → surgical referral, not rest
  - Disposition stated explicitly: orthopedic/sports-surgery referral with interim throwing/loading cessation; a stable lesion would instead be rest + serial follow-up

Model report findings:

PROTOCOL: Non-contrast 3T elbow MRI with fluid-sensitive fat-suppressed (PD/T2 FS) and T1 sequences. The capitellar lesion is interrogated on CORONAL and SAGITTAL together (coronal for the medial-lateral face, sagittal for anterior-to-posterior extent). Clinical question: capitellar OCD versus Panner, and — if OCD — stable versus unstable, because that determines rest versus surgical referral.

BONES & MARROW: There is a FOCAL subchondral signal abnormality in the ANTEROLATERAL capitellum at the radiocapitellar contact zone. The ossific nucleus is otherwise normally ossified for age, and the abnormality is discrete and lesional — NOT a diffuse low-signal involvement of the entire capitellar ossific nucleus (i.e., this is OCD, not Panner). The lesion is demarcated peripherally by a partially low-signal margin. Surrounding reactive marrow edema extends into the capitellar metaphysis; this edema is noted but is NOT by itself a stability sign. No medial epicondyle apophyseal widening to suggest a concurrent Little League (medial) lesion. The radial head, trochlea, olecranon, and coronoid are normal; no occult fracture line.

OSTEOCHONDRAL STABILITY (the management-defining read): At the lesion interface there is a T2 fluid-bright line that closely matches joint-fluid signal and COMPLETELY UNDERCUTS the osteochondral fragment, separating it from the underlying capitellum. A sizeable subchondral cyst (with a smaller satellite cyst) sits deep to the fragment. The overlying articular cartilage demonstrates a high-signal cleft that REACHES the fragment surface (a cartilage breach/delamination). The fragment remains in situ (non-displaced) but is mechanically separated by these findings. Taken together — fluid undercutting + a sizeable/multiple cyst(s) + a cartilage breach — the lesion is UNSTABLE. (No criterion here is an isolated high-T2 line alone, which would be indeterminate.)

LIGAMENTS: The anterior bundle of the UCL traces intact from the medial epicondyle to the sublime tubercle with no T-sign (no fluid tracking beyond the articular-cartilage edge at the distal attachment) — pertinent negative given the throwing history. The lateral collateral / LUCL complex and annular ligament are intact; no posterolateral instability sign.

TENDONS: Common extensor (ECRB) and common flexor-pronator origins are normal without tendinosis or tear. Distal biceps and triceps insertions are intact.

NERVES: The ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel is normal in caliber and signal relative to the median nerve and shows no flexion subluxation; no ulnar, median/AIN, or radial/PIN denervation.

JOINT: Small joint effusion. Critically, the dependent recesses (coronoid fossa, olecranon fossa, radiocapitellar recess) are swept and are CLEAR — no separate intra-articular loose body. No pathologically thickened radiocapitellar plica to mimic the OCD. The pseudodefect of the capitellum (normal posterolateral bare-area groove) is distinct from the anterolateral lesion and is not over-called.

Model report impression:

1. CAPITELLAR OSTEOCHONDRITIS DISSECANS (OCD), anterolateral capitellum, in a skeletally immature gymnast/thrower — focal and lesional, distinguishing it from Panner disease (which is a self-limited osteochondrosis of the whole ossific nucleus in a younger child).
2. The lesion is UNSTABLE — this is the management-changing line: a T2 fluid-bright line (matching joint fluid) completely undercuts the fragment, with a sizeable/multiple subchondral cyst(s) and a high-signal cartilage cleft reaching the fragment. Any ONE of these is a surgical-referral sign; here there are three.
3. Fragment is currently in situ (non-displaced) and the dependent recesses are clear of a loose body — but the undercutting/cyst/cartilage-breach pattern still places this in the unstable, surgical category.
4. Surrounding marrow edema is present but was NOT used as a stability criterion; UCL (no T-sign) and the LUCL complex are intact.
5. DISPOSITION: orthopedic/sports-surgery referral for an unstable capitellar OCD; recommend throwing/loading cessation in the interim. (Stable lesions — intact cartilage, no fluid undercutting, low-signal demarcating margin — are managed with rest and serial follow-up instead.)

Teaching points:
- OCD vs Panner is settled by AGE + FOCALITY, not severity: capitellar OCD is the older adolescent (~12-17 yr) thrower/gymnast with a FOCAL anterolateral lesion that can fragment; Panner is the younger child (~5-10 yr), involves the WHOLE ossific nucleus, is self-limited/reversible, and characteristically does NOT shed a discrete fragment or form a subchondral cyst. Get the age and the focality right before you talk stability.
- Stability is the management hinge, and it is read on CORONAL + SAGITTAL together. Call it UNSTABLE (→ surgical referral) if ANY one of: a T2 fluid-bright line completely undercutting the fragment (matching joint-fluid signal); a sizeable or multiple subchondral cyst(s); a high-signal cartilage cleft reaching the fragment; or a displaced fragment/loose body. STABLE (→ throwing rest + serial follow-up) = no surrounding fluid-bright signal, intact overlying cartilage, an in-situ fragment, and a low-signal demarcating margin.
- Two traps that change the disposition: surrounding marrow EDEMA ALONE is NOT a stability sign (it is present in both stable and unstable lesions), and an ISOLATED high-T2 line without a cartilage breach or cyst is INDETERMINATE (it can be healing granulation tissue). Don't upgrade a lesion to 'unstable' on edema, and don't let a lone interface line by itself drive surgery — get MR/CT arthrography when stability will change the operation.
- Teach the stability signs QUALITATIVELY. The 5 mm cyst figure is De Smet's KNEE-derived criterion and is NOT validated for the capitellum — say 'sizeable or multiple subchondral cysts.' Likewise the often-quoted ~0.92 sensitivity / ~0.85 specificity is OCD-wide (predominantly knee) data extrapolated to the elbow, so cite the SIGNS, not a borrowed accuracy number.
- Always sweep the dependent recesses (coronoid fossa, olecranon fossa, radiocapitellar recess) for a separate loose body — a displaced fragment/loose body is itself an instability criterion and a mechanical-symptom source — and don't mistake the normal capitellar pseudodefect (posterolateral bare-area groove) or a thickened radiocapitellar plica for the OCD lesion.

### Distal Biceps Tendon Tear (Retraction + Lacertus)

ID: `elbow-distal-biceps-tear` · Difficulty: foundational · Tier: core · Resident visible: true

Clinical scenario: A 48-year-old man felt a sudden tearing sensation in the front of the elbow while lifting a heavy box with the elbow flexed at about 90 degrees. He has antecubital ecchymosis, weak and painful resisted supination, and a positive hook test (the examiner cannot hook a finger under an intact distal biceps tendon laterally).

Key diagnoses:
- Complete distal biceps tendon tear (avulsion) from the radial tuberosity
- Bicipitoradial bursitis (reactive fluid at the footprint)
- Lacertus fibrosus status as the retraction-limiting variable (intact vs disrupted)
- Empty radial tuberosity footprint with proximal tendon retraction
Search pattern findings:
- **Step 1: Verify Protocol & Clinical Question**
  - Confirm a dedicated FABS view is present to profile the distal biceps footprint, plus axial/coronal/sagittal fluid-sensitive and T1 sequences
  - The clinical question is anterior - a distal biceps mechanism injury after a pop with weak supination and a positive hook test
  - Map the symptom to the anterior compartment (biceps), not the medial valgus triad or the lateral instability circle
- **Step 2: Bones, Marrow & Osteochondral**
  - Mild reactive marrow edema at the radial (bicipital) tuberosity at the avulsed footprint
  - No radial-head or other fracture line, no capitellar OCD, no loose body
  - No posterior fat-pad sign - argues against an occult intra-articular fracture
- **Step 3: Medial Ligament — UCL & Valgus Overload**
  - Anterior bundle of the UCL intact to the sublime tubercle with NO T-sign (pertinent negative)
  - No sublime-tubercle marrow edema or traction spur
  - This is a flexor-mechanism avulsion, not a valgus-overload injury
- **Step 4: Lateral Ligament — LCL Complex, PLRI & Coronoid**
  - LUCL, RCL, and annular ligament intact - no posterolateral rotatory instability pattern (pertinent negative)
  - Coronoid intact, no anteromedial-facet fracture
  - No radiocapitellar incongruity or post-dislocation impaction
- **Step 5: Tendons — Biceps, Triceps & Epicondyle Origins**
  - COMPLETE distal biceps tear with an EMPTY radial-tuberosity footprint and a balled-up wavy stump, profiled on FABS - the key finding
  - Retraction approximately 4 cm with a DISRUPTED lacertus fibrosus (the lacertus status and retraction distance are the management-changing variables)
  - Triceps and common flexor/extensor origins intact; bicipitoradial bursitis as a corroborating clue
- **Step 6: Nerves — Ulnar, Median / AIN, Radial / PIN**
  - Radial nerve / PIN normal at the radiocapitellar level - no compression by the distended bicipitoradial bursa (pertinent negative to check)
  - Ulnar and median nerves normal in caliber and signal
  - No muscle denervation edema
- **Step 7: Cartilage, Joint, Capsule, Bursa & Synovium**
  - Bicipitoradial bursa distended with fluid/hematoma at the footprint
  - Small radiocapitellar/ulnotrochlear effusion; cartilage preserved
  - No loose body or radiocapitellar plica
- **Step 8: Masses, Management Review & Red Flags**
  - No mass, ganglion, or marrow-replacement red flag - the distended bursa is reactive, not a tumor
  - Confirm the surgically relevant injury: a complete distal biceps avulsion with a disrupted lacertus and ~4 cm retraction
  - Single management-changing line: prompt orthopedic referral for anatomic repair; earlier is technically easier (a guideline, not a hard deadline), and a chronic irreducible tear needs graft

Model report findings:

PROTOCOL: Non-contrast elbow MRI with axial, coronal, and sagittal fluid-sensitive (T2 FS/STIR) and T1 sequences, including a dedicated FABS (flexion-abduction-supination, prone) view to lay the distal biceps footprint flat at the radial tuberosity. Clinical question: distal biceps tendon injury after a mechanical pop with weak supination and a positive hook test.

BONES & MARROW: Radial head, capitellum, trochlea, olecranon, and coronoid are intact without fracture line or osteochondral lesion. There is mild reactive marrow edema at the radial (bicipital) tuberosity at the biceps footprint, but the empty footprint is the dominant finding. No capitellar OCD, no loose body, no posterior fat-pad sign to suggest occult intra-articular fracture.

LIGAMENTS: The anterior bundle of the UCL is intact to the sublime tubercle with no T-sign. The LCL/LUCL complex and annular ligament are intact - no post-traumatic instability pattern. (These are pertinent negatives, since the injury here is a flexor mechanism avulsion, not a valgus or posterolateral instability event.)

TENDONS (DISTAL BICEPS - key region): There is full-thickness discontinuity of the distal biceps tendon at the radial-tuberosity footprint. The tuberosity footprint is EMPTY, and the torn tendon end is balled-up and wavy. On the FABS view the footprint is profiled and confirmed denuded. The stump is retracted proximally; it sits approximately 4 cm proximal to the radial tuberosity (measured from the tuberosity to the distal tendon margin on the long-axis/sagittal and FABS images). The LACERTUS FIBROSUS is DISRUPTED, which has allowed this degree of proximal retraction and the empty tuberosity. Fluid and a small hematoma fill the bicipitoradial bursa and the gap between the stump and the denuded footprint. The brachialis is intact (an important pertinent negative - it preserves some elbow flexion and can mask the deficit clinically). Triceps and common flexor/extensor origins are intact.

NERVES: The radial nerve / PIN at the radiocapitellar level and the median nerve are normal in caliber and signal; no mass or distended bursa is compressing the PIN. No muscle denervation edema.

JOINT: Small radiocapitellar/ulnotrochlear effusion. Articular cartilage preserved. No synovitis or loose body.

Model report impression:

1. COMPLETE (full-thickness) distal biceps tendon tear avulsed from the radial tuberosity, with an EMPTY footprint and a balled-up retracted stump.
2. The lacertus fibrosus is DISRUPTED, with proximal retraction of approximately 4 cm - the two management-changing variables (complete-vs-partial and retraction + lacertus status) both point toward an anatomic surgical repair, and the degree of retraction with a torn lacertus is the surgically significant feature.
3. Reactive bicipitoradial bursitis and a small footprint hematoma; intact brachialis (preserves some flexion and can mask the clinical deficit).
4. No UCL/LCL instability pattern and no PIN compression. Recommend prompt orthopedic/sports-surgery referral for distal biceps repair - earlier anatomic repair is technically easier (a guideline, not a hard 2-4-week deadline); a chronically retracted, irreducible tendon may require graft reconstruction.

Teaching points:
- The two management-changing variables in every distal biceps tear are (1) PARTIAL vs COMPLETE and (2) RETRACTION distance plus LACERTUS FIBROSUS status. Report all three explicitly: partial tears are usually undersurface at the radial-tuberosity footprint (and are managed conservatively first), while a complete tear with retraction is a surgical/anatomic repair.
- The lacertus fibrosus is the key tether. An INTACT lacertus limits retraction and can leave the stump near the radial neck despite a FULL rupture - it masks the tear clinically and on imaging (the classic missed complete tear) and changes the surgical approach. A DISRUPTED lacertus, as here, allows proximal retraction and an empty tuberosity. Always state which it is.
- Management/disposition pearl: a complete distal biceps avulsion in an active patient is a surgical referral for anatomic repair. Earlier repair is technically easier - frame timing as a GUIDELINE (earlier is better), NOT a hard 2-4-week deadline. Many primary repairs succeed out to roughly 4-6 weeks and beyond; chronic, retracted, irreducible tears that will not reach the tuberosity are the ones that need a graft.
- Pitfall: an intact brachialis preserves some elbow flexion and an intact lacertus preserves some gross strength, so the patient may not look as weak as expected - weak SUPINATION and a positive HOOK test are the more reliable clinical clues. Do not let preserved flexion talk you out of a complete tear.
- The FABS view (flexion-abduction-supination, prone) profiles the footprint and is the best plane to grade partial-vs-complete and measure retraction; bicipitoradial bursitis is a CLUE to scrutinize the insertion, not a diagnosis. A large distended bursa can also compress the PIN, so check the radial nerve.

PROTOCOL: Non-contrast 3T elbow MRI; coronal and axial fluid-sensitive fat-saturated (T2 FS) and T1 sequences. 1. Lateral epicondylitis: common extensor (ECRB) origin tendinosis with a DEEP partial-thickness undersurface tear that undercuts and communicates with the radiocapitellar joint.
2. 3. Reactive lateral-epicondyle marrow edema and thin RCL signal are present but are NOT independent evidence of an LCL tear and should not be over-called.

Teaching points:
- 'Tennis elbow' is angiofibroblastic TENDINOSIS of the common extensor origin (ECRB), not an inflammatory process — which is why corticosteroid injection gives only short-term benefit and may worsen long-term outcomes. Grade it as tendinosis vs partial-thickness vs full-thickness, and treat tendinosis/low-grade partials with load-based rehab first.
- The management-changing companion is the LUCL: trace it to the supinator crest on EVERY lateral-epicondylitis read. Anchor an LUCL/PLRI call to loss of ligament continuity, an undersurface tear deep to the common extensor, and secondary signs (posterolateral radial subluxation/radiocapitellar incongruity) plus the clinical pivot-shift — not edema alone.
- PLRI from an undercutting deep extensor/LUCL tear is a classic post-injection and post-lateral-release complication. In a patient who has had prior lateral steroid injections or a release and now has refractory or new mechanical lateral symptoms, specifically interrogate the LUCL for iatrogenic injury.

A deep extensor tear INVOLVING the LUCL with PLRI is the surgical exception — refer to orthopedics/elbow surgery for ligament repair/reconstruction. 1. Medial (common flexor-pronator) epicondylitis with a partial-thickness origin tear - thickening, intrasubstance fluid signal, and a focal deep partial tear, NOT a full-thickness avulsion.
2. 3. The anterior-bundle UCL is intact (T-sign absent) - the third member of the medial triad is cleared; no valgus-instability lesion.

Teaching points:
- The MEDIAL TRIAD: medial epicondylitis (golfer's elbow), the anterior-bundle UCL, and the ulnar nerve travel together because the cubital tunnel sits immediately posterior to the flexor-pronator origin. Calling 'medial epicondylitis' and stopping is the classic satisfaction-of-search miss.
- Anchor the ulnar-neuritis call to STRUCTURE, not signal alone: mild ulnar-nerve T2 hyperintensity occurs in asymptomatic elbows. Make the diagnosis on caliber enlargement + muscle denervation (FCU/ulnar FDP/ulnar intrinsics) + clinical correlation, using the ipsilateral median nerve and the contralateral ulnar nerve as internal controls. Denervation edema is subacute (T2/STIR) and progresses to chronic T1 fatty atrophy - name the phase.
- Management/disposition pearl: uncomplicated medial epicondylitis with ulnar neuritis is REHAB-first (swing/activity modification, flexor-pronator and kinetic-chain rehab, nerve hygiene, night extension splinting). The presence of ulnar symptoms changes the OPERATIVE plan if surgery is later needed - the nerve must be addressed, not ignored, when the flexor-pronator origin is debrided.
- The single most operative-changing nerve finding is DYNAMIC SUBLUXATION: an ulnar nerve that subluxates over the medial epicondyle in flexion (or a coexisting anconeus epitrochlearis or snapping triceps) favors selective ANTERIOR TRANSPOSITION over in-situ decompression. Assess subluxation on flexion axial images and look for the accessory muscle - it is absent here, so this nerve is stable.
- Pitfall: do not overcall the UCL. A couple of millimetres of fluid undercutting the distal UCL can be a normal synovial recess; the abnormal T-sign is fluid tracking BEYOND the articular-cartilage edge. But DO trace it every time - a concomitant UCL tear in a thrower (less so this older golfer) shifts the conversation from rehab toward UCL reconstruction, and transposition with reconstruction is selective (for preoperative ulnar symptoms/instability), not routine.

2. - Cubital tunnel syndrome is the SECOND most common compression neuropathy after carpal tunnel — and the most common at the elbow. Mild ulnar-nerve T2 hyperintensity occurs in asymptomatic elbows, so compare the nerve to the ipsilateral MEDIAN nerve (internal control) and the CONTRALATERAL ulnar nerve. - Stage the denervation by timing: there is NO MRI change for the first ~2-4 weeks, then SUBACUTE (potentially reversible) T2/STIR muscle edema in FCU and the ulnar FDP, then CHRONIC T1 fatty atrophy. Subacute edema is a subacute, not hyperacute, finding — and fatty atrophy signals later, less reversible disease that raises the urgency of decompression.

PROTOCOL: Non-contrast 3T elbow MRI of the throwing (right) arm with coronal, axial, and sagittal fluid-sensitive fat-saturated (T2 FS/STIR) and T1 sequences. Clinical question: posterior elbow pain and mechanical catching at ball release in a professional pitcher - valgus extension overload versus a loose body, with the UCL specifically in question. NOTE: sagittal fluid-sensitive images are the workhorse for clearing the dependent recesses for loose bodies; MR arthrography or a valgus-applied adjunct is reserved for if the UCL undersurface remains equivocal.

BONES & MARROW: There is a posteromedial olecranon OSTEOPHYTE projecting from the olecranon tip into the olecranon fossa, with reactive subchondral marrow edema at the posteromedial olecranon and a mirror-image (kissing) marrow change at the posteromedial trochlea/medial wall of the fossa - the impaction signature of valgus extension overload. Model report impression:

1. Valgus extension overload of the throwing elbow: posteromedial olecranon osteophyte with kissing posteromedial trochlear/olecranon-fossa chondral wear and reactive marrow edema.
2. Discrete intra-articular LOOSE BODY in the posterior compartment / olecranon fossa - the mechanical catching/locking source; the coronoid and radial fossae and radiocapitellar recess were cleared (CT is more sensitive for counting small ossified bodies pre-op).
3. CO-EXISTING UCL INSUFFICIENCY - chronic attenuation of the anterior bundle with a distal undersurface (T-sign) partial tear and a sublime-tubercle traction spur. THIS IS THE MANAGEMENT-CHANGING FINDING: the loose body and osteophyte explain the catching, but the underlying UCL laxity is the upstream driver and MUST be addressed - isolated debridement/osteophyte excision without accounting for the UCL can unmask or worsen valgus instability.
4. Low-grade ulnar neuritis without subluxation or denervation, completing the valgus-overload triad.

Teaching points:
- Valgus extension overload is a CHAIN, not a single lesion: repetitive throwing valgus overloads the UCL, and the resulting medial laxity lets the posteromedial olecranon impact the olecranon-fossa wall in terminal extension, producing the posteromedial osteophyte, kissing chondral wear, and ultimately a loose body. Read the whole chain, starting upstream at the UCL.
- The management-changing pearl: the loose body and osteophyte cause the catching, but the UCL is the keystone. Debriding the osteophyte or removing the loose body WITHOUT addressing UCL insufficiency can UNMASK or WORSEN valgus instability - and over-resecting the posteromedial olecranon itself increases UCL strain. Always grade the UCL (T-sign? full-thickness? chronic attenuation?) before signing off a 'simple loose body.'
- Hunt loose bodies systematically on SAGITTAL fluid-sensitive images, sweeping the dependent recesses - olecranon fossa (classic in VEO), coronoid fossa, and radiocapitellar recess - against bright joint fluid. Know the limit of the modality: MRI undercounts small ossified bodies, so when an exact pre-operative count matters, CT or CT arthrography is more sensitive.
- Context changes the differential for the same loose-body pattern: in a THROWER it is valgus extension overload (UCL -> posteromedial impingement -> osteophyte/loose body), but in a NON-thrower or laborer the identical olecranon-fossa loose body and tip osteophytes are usually PRIMARY OA - a different counseling and surgical conversation, without the UCL-reconstruction overlay.
- Complete the medial triad in every thrower: UCL + flexor-pronator origin + ulnar nerve travel together. Document the ulnar neuritis and any flexor-pronator tendinosis, but anchor the ulnar-nerve call to caliber change plus denervation plus clinical correlation, not mild T2 signal alone - and note that concurrent ulnar nerve transposition with UCL surgery is selective, for pre-operative ulnar symptoms or instability, not routine.

### Occult Radial Head Fracture vs Normal Variant (Pseudodefect)

ID: `elbow-occult-radial-head-fracture` · Difficulty: intermediate · Tier: core · Resident visible: true

Clinical scenario: A 30-year-old presents after a fall on the outstretched hand (FOOSH) with lateral elbow pain, tenderness over the radial head, and painful, mechanically limited forearm pronation/supination. Radiographs show a displaced posterior fat-pad sign (elbow effusion/hemarthrosis) but no definite fracture line. MRI is requested to confirm an occult fracture and exclude an osteochondral lesion.

Key diagnoses:
- Non-displaced radial head/neck fracture (occult on radiograph)
- Hemarthrosis with a displaced posterior fat-pad sign
- Capitellar pseudodefect and transverse trochlear ridge — normal variants (NOT osteochondral lesions)
- No mechanical block to forearm rotation and no comminution (non-operative Mason I pattern)
Search pattern findings:
- **Step 1: Verify Protocol & Clinical Question**
  - Confirm a non-contrast elbow MRI with T1 and fluid-sensitive (T2 FS/STIR) coronal, sagittal, and axial sequences — T1 to show the occult fracture line, fluid-sensitive to show marrow edema
  - State the exact question: occult fracture after a FOOSH with a displaced posterior fat-pad sign and no visible radiographic line
  - Map the symptom as lateral/mechanical (radial head, painful forearm rotation) to steer the search; an MR arthrogram is not needed for this question
- **Step 2: Bones, Marrow & Osteochondral**
  - Non-displaced radial head/neck FRACTURE LINE (low T1) with surrounding fat-suppressed-bright marrow edema — a true fracture, not a bone bruise (edema only)
  - Pertinent negative: capitellar pseudodefect and transverse trochlear ridge are NORMAL — not osteochondral lesions or fractures
  - Radiocapitellar line bisects the capitellum (no occult Monteggia); in a child, specifically clear the non-union-prone lateral condyle fracture
- **Step 3: Medial Ligament — UCL & Valgus Overload**
  - Anterior-bundle UCL intact to the sublime tubercle
  - No T-sign — no fluid tracking beyond the articular-cartilage edge at the distal attachment
  - No sublime-tubercle marrow edema or traction spur to suggest valgus overload
- **Step 4: Lateral Ligament — LCL Complex, PLRI & Coronoid**
  - LUCL, RCL, and annular ligament intact — no posterolateral rotatory instability after this FOOSH
  - Coronoid (including the anteromedial facet) intact — no varus posteromedial rotatory instability pattern and no terrible-triad components
  - No Osborne–Cotterill posterolateral capitellar impaction or heterotopic ossification
- **Step 5: Tendons — Biceps, Triceps & Epicondyle Origins**
  - Distal biceps and triceps insertions intact — no partial/complete tear or retraction
  - Common extensor (ECRB) and common flexor-pronator origins normal — no epicondylitis
  - No flexor-pronator or extensor muscle strain
- **Step 6: Nerves — Ulnar, Median / AIN, Radial / PIN**
  - Ulnar nerve normal in caliber/signal in the cubital tunnel; no flexion subluxation
  - No median/AIN or radial/PIN muscle denervation edema to suggest entrapment
  - Pertinent negative: a radial-head/neck fracture sits near the PIN — confirm no secondary nerve involvement
- **Step 7: Cartilage, Joint, Capsule, Bursa & Synovium**
  - Moderate joint effusion/hemarthrosis distending the capsule and displacing the posterior fat pad — the correlate of the radiographic fat-pad sign
  - Radiocapitellar and ulnotrochlear cartilage intact; no intra-articular loose body in the dependent recesses
  - No thickened radiocapitellar plica and no olecranon bursitis
- **Step 8: Masses, Management Review & Red Flags**
  - No mass/ganglion, no confluent low-T1 marrow replacement or cortical destruction — no infection/tumor red flag
  - Confirm the single management-changing point: stable non-displaced (Mason I) radial-head fracture with NO mechanical block, displaced articular fragment, or comminution → non-operative with early protected motion
  - Disposition: the operative trigger is a mechanical block to rotation, an articular fragment impeding motion, or Mason III comminution — not 2 mm alone; do not overcall the pseudodefect/trochlear ridge as a lesion

Model report findings:

PROTOCOL: Non-contrast elbow MRI with T1 and fluid-sensitive fat-suppressed (T2 FS/STIR) sequences in coronal, sagittal, and axial planes. Clinical question: occult fracture in an adult after a FOOSH with a displaced posterior fat-pad sign on radiograph and no visible fracture line; exclude an osteochondral lesion.

BONES & MARROW: There is a non-displaced, obliquely oriented FRACTURE LINE through the radial head extending into the proximal radial neck, low-signal on T1 with surrounding fat-suppressed-bright marrow EDEMA on the fluid-sensitive sequences. The line reaches the articular surface but the fragments are anatomically aligned without a step-off or depression. Importantly, this is a true fracture (a discrete LINE plus marrow edema), not merely a bone bruise (which would show edema WITHOUT a line). The capitellum, trochlea, olecranon, and coronoid show no fracture line or marrow edema. The radiocapitellar line (central long axis of the radial neck) bisects the capitellum on every plane — no radial-head subluxation/dislocation (no occult Monteggia).

NORMAL VARIANTS — DO NOT OVERCALL: The posterolateral capitellar bare-area groove (the PSEUDODEFECT of the capitellum) is seen at the capitellum–lateral-epicondyle junction and is a normal contour, NOT an osteochondral lesion — there is no fluid-undercut fragment, no subchondral cyst, and no cartilage breach to suggest capitellar OCD. The transverse TROCHLEAR RIDGE/groove is likewise a normal osseous landmark and must not be called a fracture or osteochondral defect.

LIGAMENTS: The anterior bundle of the ulnar collateral ligament is intact to the sublime tubercle with no T-sign (no fluid tracking beyond the articular-cartilage edge). The lateral ulnar collateral ligament and radial collateral/annular ligament complex are intact; no secondary signs of posterolateral rotatory instability and no posterolateral capitellar (Osborne–Cotterill) impaction.

TENDONS: Common extensor and common flexor-pronator origins are normal without tendinosis or tear. Distal biceps and triceps insertions are intact.

NERVES: Ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel is normal in caliber and signal; median/AIN and radial/PIN territories show no muscle denervation edema.

JOINT, CARTILAGE & CAPSULE: There is a moderate joint EFFUSION/hemarthrosis distending the capsule and displacing the posterior fat pad out of the olecranon fossa — the imaging correlate of the radiographic fat-pad sign. Radiocapitellar and ulnotrochlear cartilage are intact. No intra-articular loose body in the coronoid fossa, olecranon fossa, or radiocapitellar recess; no thickened radiocapitellar plica.

Model report impression:

1. Non-displaced occult RADIAL HEAD/NECK fracture (fracture line + marrow edema) with a hemarthrosis displacing the posterior fat pad — confirming the radiographic fat-pad sign. A displaced posterior fat pad after trauma with a normal radiograph is an occult intra-articular fracture (radial head most common in adults) until proven otherwise; MRI confirms it and distinguishes a true fracture (line + edema) from a bone bruise (edema only).
2. The capitellar PSEUDODEFECT and the transverse TROCHLEAR RIDGE are NORMAL VARIANTS — not osteochondral lesions; no capitellar OCD.
3. MANAGEMENT-CHANGING LINE: there is NO mechanical block to forearm rotation, no displaced articular fragment impeding motion, and no comminution — this is a stable, non-displaced (Mason I) pattern appropriate for NON-OPERATIVE management (early protected range of motion). The operative trigger would be a mechanical block to rotation, an articular fragment displaced enough to impede motion, or comminution of the whole head (Mason III) — NOT the 2 mm number alone.
4. Ligaments (UCL, LCL/LUCL) and tendons intact; no instability pattern. (In a child, the same fat pad would prompt a dedicated search for the non-union-prone lateral condyle/Salter-Harris IV fracture.)

Teaching points:
- A displaced posterior fat-pad sign with a normal radiograph is an OCCULT INTRA-ARTICULAR FRACTURE until proven otherwise — in an adult after a FOOSH, the radial head is the most common culprit. Do not 'reassure away' an effusion just because no line is visible on plain film; image it or treat it as a fracture.
- MRI earns its keep by distinguishing a TRUE FRACTURE (a discrete low-signal LINE plus marrow edema) from a BONE BRUISE (marrow edema WITHOUT a line). That distinction changes immobilization, follow-up, and counseling on healing time.
- The don't-overcall counterpoint: the capitellar PSEUDODEFECT (posterolateral bare-area groove) and the transverse TROCHLEAR RIDGE are normal variants — calling either an osteochondral lesion or fracture sends a patient down an unnecessary surgical pathway. Capitellar OCD requires a fluid-undercut fragment, a sizeable/multiple subchondral cyst (the 5 mm figure is knee-derived, not validated for the capitellum), a cartilage breach, or a displaced fragment — none of which a pseudodefect has.
- The radial-head SURGICAL TRIGGER is functional, not a single number: a mechanical BLOCK to forearm rotation, an articular fragment displaced enough to impede motion, or comminution of the whole head (Mason III). The '2 mm' figure alone does not mandate surgery — many minimally displaced Mason I/II fractures without a block are managed non-operatively with early motion.
- Run the radiocapitellar line on every projection/plane (it must bisect the capitellum) to avoid missing an occult Monteggia, and in CHILDREN convert the same fat-pad sign into a dedicated hunt for the non-union-prone lateral condyle (Salter-Harris IV) fracture — a classic pediatric miss with very different stakes.

### Structure Pearls / Readings / Correlates

- **Capitellum:** Watch for an osteochondral lesion of the capitellum — judge stability on the interface: a fluid line completely undercutting the fragment, a sizeable/multiple cyst, a cartilage breach, or a displaced fragment = unstable. Surrounding edema alone is not, and an isolated high-T2 line is indeterminate.
- **Radial head:** Watch for an occult radial-head fracture after a fall — a marrow-edema line with a displaced posterior fat pad; the surgical trigger is a mechanical block to forearm rotation or comminution (Mason III), not the 2 mm number alone.
- **Ulnar collateral ligament (anterior bundle):** Watch for the T-sign — fluid undercutting the distal UCL beyond the articular-cartilage edge — which supports a thrower's partial undersurface tear in the right clinical setting; MR arthrography is the most sensitive study, but symptoms and laxity still matter.
- **Common flexor-pronator origin:** Watch the medial 'valgus triad' together: the flexor-pronator origin, the UCL to the sublime tubercle, and the ulnar nerve just posterior — valgus overload injures all three.
- **Common extensor origin:** Watch the LUCL deep to the extensor origin: a deep common-extensor tear that takes the LUCL implies posterolateral rotatory instability — a ligament-reconstruction problem, not a debridement, and a classic post-injection/post-release complication.
- **Ulnar nerve (cubital tunnel):** Watch for a structural cause that can change operative planning — dynamic flexion subluxation of the nerve, an anconeus epitrochlearis, or a snapping triceps may favor transposition over in-situ decompression.

- **conf-elbow-tendons-biceps-triceps-epicondylitis (elbow-tendons-biceps-triceps-epicondylitis):** I can grade the distal biceps and triceps tendons for partial versus complete tear (with retraction and the lacertus fibrosus), grade lateral and medial epicondylitis, and always check the adjacent ligament (LUCL with lateral, UCL with medial).
- **conf-elbow-nerves-entrapment (elbow-nerves-entrapment):** I can assess the ulnar, median/AIN, and radial/PIN nerves by caliber and signal against an internal control, use muscle denervation as the localizing sign, recognize ulnar-nerve subluxation and snapping triceps, and consider Parsonage–Turner before calling mechanical compression.
- **conf-elbow-dont-miss (elbow-dont-miss):** I can prioritize the management-changing elbow findings — complete distal biceps avulsion, unstable capitellar OCD, the thrower's T-sign/full-thickness UCL tear, a common-extensor tear involving the LUCL, and the anteromedial coronoid facet fracture — and route each to the correct disposition.

## Red Flags

- **Complete distal biceps avulsion (or a complete tear with an intact lacertus that doesn't retract):** A time-sensitive surgical repair (≈40–50% supination / ≈30% flexion strength lost). An intact lacertus tethers the tendon so it neither retracts nor shows an obvious clinical gap — the classic undercall as 'partial.' Report partial-vs-complete, retraction in cm, and lacertus integrity, and refer promptly while the tendon still reaches the tuberosity.
- **Unstable capitellar OCD:** A fluid line undercutting the fragment, sizeable/multiple subchondral cysts, a cartilage breach, or a displaced fragment/loose body in a young thrower → stop throwing and refer for surgery. Surrounding edema alone is not instability; an isolated high-T2 line is indeterminate.
- **Thrower's UCL tear — the deep-fiber T-sign or a full-thickness tear:** The undersurface (sublime-tubercle) partial tear is occult to exam and at arthroscopy and ends a throwing season if missed; distal/full-thickness tears do worse nonoperatively. Name surface, location, and acute-vs-chronic; add MR arthrography when non-contrast MRI is equivocal.
- **Deep common-extensor tear involving the LUCL (posterolateral rotatory instability):** A deep ECRB tear with a fluid-bright gap undercutting the radiocapitellar joint and involving the LUCL turns a routine 'tennis elbow' debridement into a ligament repair/reconstruction (a classic post-injection/post-release complication). Trace the LUCL to the supinator crest before signing off any high-grade lateral epicondylitis.
- **Anteromedial coronoid facet fracture (and the terrible triad):** The small-looking anteromedial facet fragment implies varus posteromedial rotatory instability and is surgical; the terrible triad (dislocation + radial head + coronoid) is unstable with the coronoid and LCL as keystones. Classify the coronoid, look for the Osborne–Cotterill lesion on any post-dislocation elbow, and route to surgery (CT maps the fragments).

## Quick Reference

### anatomySections

#### Planes — what each one answers
- **Axial:** Cross-sections the four compartments. Best for the ulnar nerve in the cubital tunnel (and dynamic flexion subluxation), the radial nerve/PIN at the radiocapitellar level, the common flexor/extensor origins, the annular ligament around the radial neck, and the distal biceps footprint.
- **Coronal:** The UCL anterior bundle to the sublime tubercle, the RCL/LUCL complex, the common flexor and extensor origins, and the capitellar/trochlear articular surfaces. This is the plane to read the T-sign conceptually, while remembering that MR arthrography is more sensitive for subtle undersurface tears.
- **Sagittal:** The distal biceps and triceps, the brachialis, the anterior/posterior fat pads, the trochlea/capitellum cartilage contour, and the olecranon/coronoid fossae for loose bodies. Read capitellar OCD on coronal + sagittal together.
- **FABS (Flexion–ABduction–Supination, prone):** A dedicated view that lays the distal biceps footprint flat at the radial tuberosity for partial-tear grading and retraction measurement.

#### Sequences — what they're for
- **Fluid-sensitive fat-sat (T2 FS / STIR):** Edema, partial/complete tendon and ligament tears, the OCD fluid rim, marrow injury, effusions, and subacute muscle denervation edema. A finding is more convincing when fluid-bright signal points to it.
- **T1-weighted:** Anatomy, marrow replacement (confluent low-T1 below muscle signal is the red flag), arthrogram contrast, occult fracture lines, and chronic fatty muscle atrophy.
- **MR arthrography:** Most sensitive for the UCL undersurface (T-sign) tear and intra-articular loose bodies/chondral delamination — intra-articular contrast undercuts a partial tear and outlines bodies. An abduction/valgus or flexed positioning adjunct can help open the medial joint (the elbow-specific evidence base is thin, unlike the shoulder ABER). Add it only when it changes the decision.

#### Medial compartment
- **UCL (ulnar collateral) complex:** The anterior bundle is the primary valgus restraint (origin anteroinferior medial epicondyle → insertion at the sublime tubercle of the coronoid); the posterior bundle (cubital tunnel floor) and transverse/Cooper ligament are secondary. Normal anterior bundle = thin, taut, uniformly low signal to the sublime tubercle; a small synovial recess can be normal.
- **Common flexor-pronator origin:** Uniform low-signal tendon off the medial epicondyle; the site of medial epicondylitis. It overlies the UCL — read the two together.
- **Ulnar nerve:** In the cubital tunnel posterior to the medial epicondyle beneath Osborne's retinaculum. Assess caliber/signal and flexion subluxation; compare to the median nerve and the contralateral side.

#### Lateral compartment
- **Common extensor origin (ECRB):** Off the lateral epicondyle; the site of lateral epicondylitis (angiofibroblastic tendinosis).
- **RCL / LUCL complex:** The radial collateral ligament blends into the annular ligament; the lateral ulnar collateral ligament (LUCL: lateral epicondyle → posterior to the radial head → supinator crest) is the key restraint to posterolateral rotatory instability. The LUCL/RCL origin lies deep to the common extensor tendon — a deep extensor tear can take it.
- **Annular ligament & radiocapitellar joint:** The annular ligament stabilizes the radial head (best on axial); the capitellum is the OCD site (anterolateral/central).
- **Radial nerve / PIN:** Divides at the radiocapitellar level; the posterior interosseous nerve enters the supinator under the arcade of Frohse.
- **Pseudodefect of the capitellum:** A normal posterolateral/postero-inferior bare-area groove — NOT an osteochondral lesion. Do not over-call it.

#### Anterior, posterior compartments & coronoid
- **Distal biceps tendon:** Bifurcated footprint on the radial tuberosity; the lacertus fibrosus is the aponeurotic expansion that tethers it; the bicipitoradial bursa sits between the tendon and the tuberosity.
- **Brachialis:** Inserts on the coronoid/ulnar tuberosity; the primary elbow flexor, lying behind the biceps.
- **Triceps tendon:** Inserts broadly on the olecranon; the deep central fibers can tear first while the superficial expansion stays intact.
- **Olecranon, coronoid & fossae:** The valgus-extension-overload posteromedial olecranon osteophyte and loose-body niches; the coronoid (sublime tubercle medially, anteromedial facet laterally) is the keystone of post-traumatic stability.
- **Fat pads:** The anterior fat pad is normally seen (elevation = the sail sign); the posterior fat pad is normally hidden in the olecranon fossa — any visible posterior fat pad after trauma means effusion/hemarthrosis.

#### Normal variants — don't overcall
- **Capitellar pseudodefect & trochlear groove:** The posterolateral capitellar bare-area groove and the transverse trochlear ridge/groove are normal — not osteochondral lesions.
- **Distal-UCL synovial recess:** A small normal synovial recess sits near the distal UCL. The abnormal T-sign threshold is fluid tracking BEYOND the articular-cartilage edge — a couple of millimetres of undercutting can be normal.
- **Mild ulnar-nerve T2 signal:** Mild ulnar-nerve T2 hyperintensity occurs in asymptomatic elbows — anchor the diagnosis to caliber change + muscle denervation + clinical correlation, not signal alone.
- **Reactive epicondyle edema / thin RCL:** Reactive lateral-epicondyle marrow edema and thin RCL signal accompany lateral epicondylitis — do not over-call an LCL tear.
- **CRITOE ossification centers:** Skeletally immature secondary ossification centers (capitellum, radial head, internal/medial epicondyle, trochlea, olecranon, external/lateral epicondyle) — do not call Panner or OCD on a normal center.

### measurementSections

#### UCL grading & the T-sign
- **Grading: sprain → partial undersurface → full-thickness:** Sprain/low-grade: periligamentous/intrasubstance edema, fibers grossly intact, no surface-reaching defect. Partial undersurface tear = the T-sign (below). Full-thickness: fiber discontinuity across the whole thickness, a gap/retraction, or medial extravasation. Always describe location (proximal/mid/distal) and acute vs chronic. A <50%/>50% partial call is a descriptive heuristic, not a validated graded scale.
- **The T-sign (partial undersurface tear):** Fluid/contrast undermines the distal/sublime-tubercle attachment BEYOND the articular-cartilage edge while the proximal fibers stay attached (articular-sided, not full-thickness). The abnormal threshold is fluid extending past the cartilage margin — a couple of millimetres can be a normal recess. MR arthrography is the most sensitive study.
- **Management & the dynamic-US threshold:** Repair ± internal brace vs reconstruction is driven by location/surface/acute-vs-chronic + athlete demand + the moving-valgus exam — NOT a single MRI cutoff. Distal/full-thickness tears do worse nonoperatively. On dynamic valgus-stress ultrasound, a side-to-side medial gapping difference of ~>1 mm (commonly cited ~1.5–2 mm) is the functional threshold.

#### Capitellar OCD — stability criteria
- **Stable (rest + follow-up):** No fluid-bright surrounding signal, intact overlying cartilage, an in-situ fragment, and a low-signal demarcating margin → throwing rest and serial follow-up.
- **Unstable — any of (surgical referral):** A T2 fluid-bright line completely undercutting the fragment (matching joint-fluid signal); a sizeable or multiple subchondral cyst(s); a high-signal cartilage cleft reaching the fragment; or a displaced fragment/loose body. NOTE: the 5 mm cyst figure is a knee-derived (De Smet) criterion not validated for the capitellum — teach 'sizeable or multiple.'
- **Not stability signs / indeterminate:** Surrounding marrow edema alone is NOT a stability sign; an isolated high-T2 line without a cartilage breach or cyst is INDETERMINATE. Read on coronal + sagittal; add MR/CT arthrography when stability will change the operation.
- **OCD vs Panner:** OCD = adolescent (~12–17 yr), focal anterolateral capitellum, fragmentation possible. Panner = osteochondrosis age ~5–10 yr, the whole ossific nucleus, self-limited, no fragment/cyst.

#### Medial epicondyle avulsion & olecranon stress (young thrower)
- **Little League elbow (apophysitis):** A widened, fluid-bright medial epicondyle physis with marrow edema; managed with throwing rest.
- **Avulsion — operative triggers:** Report exact displacement in mm and whether the fragment is intra-articular/incarcerated (a transiently dislocated elbow can trap it after spontaneous reduction). Operative triggers in a throwing athlete: displacement ≥5 mm, an intra-articular/incarcerated fragment, or valgus instability — incarceration/instability mandates surgery regardless of the mm (the general-population mm cutoff is debated).
- **Olecranon stress + CRITOE:** A discrete low-signal line + edema = stress fracture; edema only = stress reaction. The olecranon apophysis fuses mid-to-late teens (~15–17 yr, later in throwers) — compare to the contralateral side, not a hard cutoff. CRITOE rule: the trochlea never ossifies before the medial epicondyle, so a visible trochlea without the medial epicondyle implies the epicondyle is avulsed (often intra-articular).

#### Distal biceps tear — retraction & the lacertus
- **Partial vs complete:** Partial = usually an undersurface tear at the radial-tuberosity footprint (best on FABS). Complete = full discontinuity with a balled-up, wavy stump.
- **Retraction & the lacertus fibrosus:** State retraction (where the stump sits, in cm) and the lacertus status. An INTACT lacertus tethers the tendon, limits retraction, and can mask a complete tear clinically and on imaging; a DISRUPTED lacertus allows proximal retraction and an empty tuberosity. Bicipitoradial bursitis is a clue to scrutinize the insertion, not a diagnosis.
- **Surgical timing (a guideline, not a deadline):** Earlier anatomic repair is technically easier; chronic retracted tears that won't reach the tuberosity (often only after many weeks-to-months, when irreducible) need a graft. Many primary repairs succeed out to ~4–6 weeks and beyond — do NOT teach a hard 2–4 week / graft-after-6-week cutoff.

#### Triceps tear
- **Tendinosis vs partial vs complete:** The deep central fibers fail first while the superficial expansion stays intact — so it can look partial despite near-complete clinical weakness. Complete = a full-thickness gap with a retracted stump ± an olecranon flake. Report the percentage of tendon width torn and the gap.
- **When to repair:** A complete rupture is surgical; high-grade partials (commonly >50% width torn, or ANY tear with extension weakness against resistance) are repaired — don't undercall as 'tendinosis.'

#### Epicondylitis & the adjacent ligament
- **Grading:** Tendinosis (thickening + intermediate signal, intact fibers, no surface-reaching fluid/gap) → partial-thickness tear → full-thickness gap at the origin. Lateral = common extensor (ECRB); medial = common flexor/pronator.
- **The companion to always check:** Trace the LUCL to the supinator crest with lateral epicondylitis, and the anterior-bundle UCL with medial epicondylitis — the adjacent ligament is where the surgery hides.
- **Escalation:** A deep/full-thickness common-extensor tear with a fluid-bright gap that undercuts/communicates with the radiocapitellar joint and INVOLVES the LUCL implies PLRI → ligament repair/reconstruction (a known complication of prior lateral steroid injection/release). A concomitant UCL tear with medial epicondylitis shifts a thrower toward UCL reconstruction. Don't over-call reactive epicondyle edema or thin RCL signal as an LCL tear.

#### LCL complex, PLRI & the coronoid (post-trauma instability)
- **LUCL & PLRI:** LUCL: lateral epicondyle → posterior to the radial head → supinator crest; the primary restraint to posterolateral rotatory instability. Trace it on coronal + axial (partial-volume averaging causes misses). There is no standardized single MRI measurement — call it on continuity, surface, and radiocapitellar alignment plus the clinical pivot-shift.
- **The O'Driscoll circle:** A dislocation disrupts soft tissue lateral-to-medial (LUCL → capsule → MCL), so the LCL is the primary lesion in most simple dislocations. Look for the Osborne–Cotterill posterolateral capitellar impaction and heterotopic ossification after any dislocation.
- **Coronoid classifications & surgical patterns:** Regan–Morrey by HEIGHT (I = tip avulsion, II = ≤50%, III = >50%); O'Driscoll for the ANTEROMEDIAL FACET (the VPMRI lesion). Persistent/recurrent PLRI → LUCL repair/reconstruction; the anteromedial coronoid facet fracture (small on radiographs) implies varus posteromedial rotatory instability and is surgical; the terrible triad (dislocation + radial head + coronoid fracture) is surgical with the coronoid and LCL as keystones. Comment on LUCL integrity in EVERY post-dislocation or post-lateral-surgery elbow.

#### Ulnar nerve / cubital tunnel
- **What to report:** Nerve enlargement and T2 hyperintensity at/just distal to the medial epicondyle, plus denervation of FCU, the ulnar FDP, and the ulnar intrinsics. Compare caliber/signal to the median nerve and the contralateral side (no universal cross-sectional-area cutoff; mild T2 brightness can be normal). Assess flexion subluxation on axial; look for an anconeus epitrochlearis, ganglion, or osteophyte. Snapping triceps can coexist.
- **What changes the operation:** Anchor the diagnosis to caliber change + muscle denervation + clinical correlation, not signal alone. Dynamic subluxation or an accessory muscle favors transposition over in-situ decompression; snapping triceps means the triceps must be addressed, not just the nerve.

#### PIN / radial tunnel & AIN
- **PIN syndrome:** The PIN compresses most commonly at the arcade of Frohse. PIN syndrome = motor digit drop with radially-deviated wrist extension (ECRL spared) and extensor-compartment denervation; the SUPINATOR is variably involved/often spared (its branches arise proximal to the arcade) — don't assert supinator denervation as a constant feature. Radial tunnel syndrome = pain without a motor deficit and often a normal MRI; report any mass at the arcade.
- **AIN (Kiloh–Nevin) — and the Parsonage–Turner caveat:** Pure motor: FPL, the FDP to index/middle, and pronator quadratus (classically isolated PQ edema). An AIN palsy is frequently neuralgic amyotrophy (Parsonage–Turner) rather than mechanical compression → usually NO surgery; pivot to EMG/observation.
- **Denervation timeline:** No MRI change for the first ~2–4 weeks → subacute T2/STIR muscle edema → chronic T1 fatty atrophy. Use the denervated muscle to localize the lesion.

#### Occult fracture & alignment
- **The displaced posterior fat pad:** Any visible (displaced) posterior fat pad in an adult after trauma = hemarthrosis from an occult intra-articular fracture (radial head most common; supracondylar and the non-union-prone lateral condyle/Salter-Harris IV in children) until proven otherwise. MRI shows the line + marrow edema and distinguishes a true fracture from a bone bruise.
- **Radiocapitellar line:** A line through the central long axis of the radial neck must bisect the capitellum on EVERY projection — failure = radial head subluxation/dislocation (think occult Monteggia).
- **Mason radial-head framework & the surgical trigger:** Mason I = non-/minimally displaced, II = displaced (commonly >2 mm) partial-articular, III = comminuted whole-head. The surgical trigger is a mechanical BLOCK to forearm rotation, an articular fragment displaced enough to impede motion, or comminution (Mason III) — NOT the 2 mm number alone (many Mason II without a block are managed nonoperatively).

#### Loose bodies, radiocapitellar plica & olecranon bursitis
- **Loose bodies:** They migrate to the dependent recesses (coronoid fossa, olecranon fossa, radiocapitellar recess) and are best seen against bright joint fluid on sagittal fluid-sensitive images; GRE blooms ossified bodies; CT/CT arthrography is most sensitive for small ossified bodies and for counting them pre-op. Primary elbow OA (coronoid/olecranon-tip and fossa osteophytes, chondral loss) is the most common source in non-throwers.
- **Radiocapitellar plica:** A thickened radiocapitellar plica/posterolateral synovial fold causes lateral catching and mimics a loose body/OCD — considered pathologic when thickened beyond ~3 mm with mechanical symptoms.
- **Olecranon bursitis:** Aseptic vs septic — MRI cannot reliably exclude infection. A red/warm/fluctuant bursa needs ASPIRATION, not just imaging.

#### Protocol — when each study changes management
- **Non-contrast MRI:** The first study for most questions: tendons, OCD, nerves, occult fracture, loose bodies. Non-contrast 3T answers most cases.
- **MR arthrography:** Add it for a suspected UCL undersurface (T-sign) tear and for loose-body/chondral-delamination detection when non-contrast MRI is equivocal, or when OCD stability will change the operation.
- **CT, radiograph & dynamic ultrasound:** CT is best for osseous fragment/loose-body quantification and complex fracture mapping (coronoid, terrible triad). Radiograph is first-line for the fat-pad sign, the radiocapitellar line, and osteophytes; dynamic ultrasound confirms ulnar-nerve subluxation and UCL valgus gapping that static MRI cannot.

## Daily Pearls

- **1:** Edema is the smoke — find the fire: tie every elbow marrow edema to a fracture line, a physis, a subchondral cyst, or an osteochondral fragment before moving on.
- **2:** Capitellar OCD instability is about the interface: a fluid line undercutting the fragment, a sizeable/multiple cyst, a cartilage breach, or a displaced fragment = unstable. Surrounding edema alone is not, and an isolated high-T2 line is indeterminate.
- **3:** Panner is the whole young capitellum (~5–10 yr) and it heals; OCD is the focal teenage capitellum (~12–17 yr) and it doesn't always.
- **4:** 'Trochlea before medial epicondyle is impossible' — if you see the trochlea but not the medial epicondyle on a child's elbow, the epicondyle is avulsed, often into the joint (CRITOE).
- **5:** A displaced posterior fat pad after adult elbow trauma is a fracture until disproven — radial head first; the surgical trigger is a block to forearm rotation, not the 2 mm number alone.
- **6:** The T-sign — fluid undermining the distal UCL beyond the articular-cartilage edge — is the partial undersurface tear missed at exam and arthroscopy; MR arthrography is the most sensitive study. (Distal tears are important and under-recognized, but don't claim they outnumber proximal tears.)
- **7:** Medial elbow pain is a triad: read the flexor-pronator origin, the UCL to the sublime tubercle, AND the ulnar nerve together — they injure together in valgus overload.
- **8:** Read lateral epicondylitis? Trace the LUCL to the supinator crest. Read medial epicondylitis? Trace the UCL. The adjacent ligament is where the surgery hides.
- **9:** The lacertus decides whether a complete distal biceps tear retracts — always report complete-vs-partial AND lacertus-intact-vs-torn; an intact lacertus is the classic mimic of a partial tear.
- **10:** Localize a nerve by its muscle: isolated pronator quadratus edema = AIN (consider Parsonage–Turner, not surgery); extensor compartment = PIN at the arcade of Frohse; FCU / ulnar-FDP / intrinsics = ulnar at the cubital tunnel — and compare the ulnar nerve to the median nerve and the other side.
- **11:** In the post-dislocation elbow the LCL is usually the primary lesion (O'Driscoll lateral-to-medial circle); chase the coronoid too — a small anteromedial facet fracture means varus posteromedial rotatory instability and is surgical.
